Remaining challenges include reducing costs and optimizing in vivo circulation of platelet products. Initial implementation will likely focus on high-value applications, such as transfusion for alloimmunized patients, before broader adoption. The long-term goal is to replace the vulnerable donor-dependent system with on-demand production of standardized, pathogen-free, and universally compatible platelets. With over 2 million platelet units transfused annually in the United States alone and recent national survey data indicating continued increases in platelet transfusions, ensuring adequate platelet availability represents a growing global healthcare challenge [ 2 , 3 ]. The complete dependence of the current system on voluntary donors creates vulnerabilities that compromise patient care. The short shelf-life of platelets (5–7 days at room temperature) necessitates continuous donor recruitment, creates substantial wastage (10%–20% of collected units expire unused), and renders stockpiling impossible [ 4 ]. Beyond logistical constraints, clinical limitations further compound the challenge. A substantial proportion of chronically transfused patients develop platelet transfusion refractoriness (PTR), often following alloimmunization against HLAs, rendering standard transfusions ineffective and compatible donors difficult to recruit [ 5 , 6 ]. Room-temperature storage elevates bacterial contamination risk, the main infectious complication in transfusion medicine [ 7 ]. Geographic disparities in donor availability and disruptions from pandemics or natural disasters highlight the fragility of the donor-dependent paradigm [ 8 ]. Ex vivo platelet generation from stem cells has emerged as a transformative solution that could restructure transfusion medicine [ 9 ]. This technology promises to create an unlimited, pathogen-free, on-demand platelet supply that eliminates donor dependence while enabling genetic engineering to produce universal, immune-compatible products [ 10 – 12 ]. After decades of foundational research into megakaryopoiesis and thrombopoiesis, a pivotal inflection point has been reached: the first human patients have received transfusions of stem cell-derived platelets in clinical trials, marking the transition from laboratory concept to clinical reality [ 13 ]. Reaching this milestone required systematic optimization of both cellular starting materials and manufacturing technologies. Initial efforts using hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) were constrained by their rarity and limited expansion capacity [ 14 ]. Human embryonic stem cells (hESCs) demonstrated scalability potential but faced ethical barriers and immunological challenges [ 15 ]. The 2006 technological breakthrough enabling the generation of induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs; adult cells reprogrammed to pluripotency) revolutionized the field by combining unlimited expansion potential with ethical acceptability and genetic tractability [ 16 ]. The development of immortalized megakaryocyte progenitor cell lines (imMKCLs) through controlled expression of pro-proliferative genes created a robust, scalable manufacturing platform centered on master cell banks [ 17 , 18 ]. Parallel advances in bioreactor design, particularly the recognition that fluid shear stress drives platelet release, enabled generation of over 100 billion platelets from imMKCLs cultured at 1×10 5 –2×10 5 cells/mL using an 8-L liquid culture bioreactor with a 6-day maturation phase [ 19 ]. However, clinical translation demands more than biotechnological feasibility. Success requires navigating complex regulatory pathways, establishing rigorous QC frameworks, achieving cost-effective industrial-scale manufacturing, and demonstrating clinical efficacy comparable to donor-derived platelets [ 20 – 22 ]. Multiple biotechnology companies and national initiatives are racing to overcome these challenges, with diverse strategic approaches ranging from autologous patient-specific products to universal donor platforms generated through genetic engineering to reduce immunogenicity [ 23 ]. We comprehensively reviewed the current state and future trajectory of ex vivo platelet production. We describe the evolution of stem cell sources and technological innovations driving manufacturing advances; detail laboratory assessment methods used for QC and characterization; analyze pioneering clinical trials and their outcomes; evaluate the corporate landscape and strategic approaches being pursued; and discuss the regulatory, economic, and scalability challenges that will determine whether this technology can revolutionize transfusion medicine ( Fig. 1 ). HSCs, identified based on CD34 cell-surface marker expression, represent the natural in vivo precursors to all blood cells and can be isolated from bone marrow, umbilical cord blood, or mobilized peripheral blood [ 24 , 25 ]. Although HSCs can directly differentiate into megakaryocytes (MKs) during short culture periods, biological constraints prevent their industrial-scale production [ 25 ]. HSCs constitute less than 0.1% of nucleated cells in the circulation, exhibit poor ex vivo self-renewal capacity, and differentiate rather than proliferate, quickly exhausting the progenitor pool [ 26 ]. Although umbilical cord blood-derived HSCs show higher proliferative potential, they often do not develop into high-ploidy MKs necessary for efficient platelet production [ 27 ]. hESCs offered a solution for scalability owing to their pluripotency and indefinite self-renewal capacity, demonstrating that large-scale production was theoretically possible [ 28 ]. However, ethical controversies over embryo destruction, limited source availability, allogeneic immune rejection risk, and inefficient early differentiation protocols have limited their clinical translation [ 15 ]. The 2006 discovery of iPSCs by Yamanaka’s group marked a major defining moment, demonstrating that adult somatic cells can be reprogrammed to an embryonic-like pluripotent state by introducing OCT4 , SOX2 , KLF4 , and c-MYC [ 16 ]. iPSCs retain the advantages of hESCs, including indefinite expansion and pluripotency, while circumventing ethical barriers and source limitations [ 29 ]. Their genetic tractability enabled the generation of patient-specific autologous platelets and “universal donor” cell lines via CRISPR-associated protein 9 (Cas9)-mediated knockout of immunogenic genes such as B2M (encoding β2-microglobulin), producing “stealth” platelets that evaded HLA-mediated clearance and NK-cell responses [ 11 , 12 ]. Despite challenges, including tumorigenicity risks from residual undifferentiated cells [ 30 ] and complex, multi-step differentiation protocols requiring rigorous QC, iPSCs have become the leading candidate for clinical translation. Alternative approaches include adipose tissue-derived stromal cells (ASCs), which can differentiate into MKs and platelets through secretion of endogenous thrombopoietin (TPO) [ 31 ], and direct conversion of fibroblasts into MK progenitors using six transcription factors ( GATA2 , RUNX1 , GATA1 , TAL1 , LMO2 , and c-MYC ) [ 32 ]. However, these approaches remain less established for scalable clinical manufacturing of platelet products than current iPSC-based approaches. Technological advances have helped optimize both genetic programming and physical culture environments. Cytokine-based protocols have been refined through better understanding of hematopoiesis, with small molecules (such as CHIR99021) and activin A improving early stage efficiency. Additionally, TA-316 (a TPO mimetic), StemRegenin 1 (SR1), ricolinostat, and dual-specificity tyrosine phosphorylation-regulated kinase 1A (DYRK1A) inhibitors offer cost-effective alternatives that enhance MK maturation and yield [ 33 – 36 ]. A pivotal breakthrough was the development of imMKCLs via inducible expression of c-MYC , BMI1 , and BCL-XL , enabling a robust two-stage manufacturing system based on good manufacturing practice (GMP)-grade master cell banks with inducible differentiation capacity [ 18 ]. More recent silencing or knockout strategies targeting cell-cycle regulators such as p53 and CDKN1A , in combination with oncogene overexpression, could overcome premature senescence and offer potentially effective immortalization of the master cell lines [ 37 , 38 ]. Genetic engineering might also enable the development of immune-evasive iPSC-derived platelets to reduce alloimmune clearance, as well as engineered platelets for targeted delivery. However, optimal immune-evasion strategies and their impacts on platelet function and safety remain unresolved [ 39 , 40 ]. Importantly, compatibility is influenced not only by HLA, but also by non-HLA alloantigens and platelet-specific antigens (e.g., human platelet antigen system), which can drive antibody-mediated clearance even without HLA mismatch, representing a key challenge for universal platelet therapies [ 41 , 42 ]. The recognition that thrombopoiesis is driven by biophysical forces transformed bioreactor design, shifting the field from static culture systems to turbulence-based platforms capable of producing near-therapeutic platelet yields [ 19 , 43 ]. However, large-scale manufacturing still faces fluid-dynamic limitations that require improved vessel design [ 44 ]. Future progress will likely depend on full automation and real-time process control to improve reproducibility and scalability, whereas material and structural features are better viewed as enabling, platform-dependent design choices rather than strict requirements [ 45 , 46 ]. Among emerging technologies, data-driven optimization based on artificial intelligence and multi-omics integration is particularly well suited to support near-term clinical translation, whereas other approaches (such as platelet cryopreservation or lyophilization) are constrained by unresolved biological and functional limitations [ 47 ]. Amidst these developments, manufacturing costs remain a major determinant of commercialization, with GMP-grade media and cytokines, as well as facility and labor, being dominant cost drivers in GMP process analyses of ex vivo platelet production [ 48 ]. However, platform-specific approaches that include master cell banking to improve reproducibility and recombinant TPO substitutes to improve stability and lower production costs are being developed [ 49 ]. Cellular characterization and purity assessment The first QC step is to confirm the identity and purity of cell populations, from MK progenitors to the final platelet product. Flow cytometry is indispensable for immunophenotyping in this process. An MK panel is used to identify and quantify cells of interest. The integrin complex αIIbβ3, composed of CD41a (αIIb) and CD61 (β3), is a definitive marker expressed throughout MK development from early progenitors to mature platelets, with levels increasing during maturation [ 33 ]. The glycoprotein Ib-IX-V complex, particularly its alpha chain CD42b (GPIbα), marks more mature MKs and platelets and is crucial for their adhesive function [ 50 ]. Other markers, such as the fatty acid translocase CD36 and the chemokine receptor CXCR4, are used to further characterize cell populations [ 51 , 52 ]. MK maturation is commonly assessed by ploidy, utilizing DNA-binding fluorescent dyes, such as Hoechst 33342 or propidium iodide. These analyses enable precise quantification of MK distributions across different ploidy classes (e.g., 2N, 4N, 8N, 16N, and higher), providing a metric of endomitosis efficiency [ 53 , 54 ]. Imaging flow cytometry (IFC) overcomes key limitations of conventional flow cytometry in MK analysis, where the rarity, large size, and irregular morphology of MKs can lead to misidentification as doublets or clumps. By combining single-cell imaging with quantitative fluorescence and morphological features, IFC improves accurate identification of true single MKs and exclusion of artifacts such as platelet-adhered leukocytes [ 55 ]. In ex vivo platelet-production workflows, a compact immunophenotyping panel provides a high-yield readout of the lineage identity, maturation state, and residual impurities. Platelet and MK lineage assignment is commonly based on CD41a and CD61 expression, with CD42a and CD42b used to refine maturation toward a platelet-like phenotype. Off-target differentiation and safety-relevant carryover can be quantified using complementary exclusion markers, including SSEA4 for residual undifferentiated pluripotent cells, CD34 for immature hematopoietic progenitors, CD45 for leukocytic contaminants, CD14 for monocyte-lineage cells, and CD235a with CD71 for erythroid contamination [ 23 , 33 , 46 ]. Molecular profiling complements surface immunophenotyping by defining the internal identity and purity of platelet products. Comprehensive proteomic analysis has identified almost 4,000 proteins in human platelets [ 56 ], whereas transcriptomic analysis through RNA-sequencing has characterized the platelet transcriptome, revealing the molecular complexity of these anucleate cells [ 57 ]. Leveraging these proteomic and transcriptomic benchmarks, recent multi-omics analyses indicate that iPSC-derived MKs share substantial overlap in expressed genes and platelet-relevant regulatory features with peripheral blood platelets [ 58 ]. Concurrently, high-depth sequencing serves as a sensitive purity screen by detecting lineage-specific RNA-expression profiles and excluding contaminating cell populations, mirroring approaches used to define the molecular signatures of human progenitor subtypes [ 59 ]. In vitro platelet function tests Beyond confirming identity, demonstrating that manufactured platelets are biologically functional and capable of performing their normal hemostatic roles requires several laboratory assays to probe different aspects of platelet physiology ( Table 1 ). Adhesion and spreading assays test the initial step of hemostasis, wherein platelets adhere to damaged vessel walls. This function is tested in vitro by measuring the ability of manufactured platelets to bind surfaces coated with extracellular matrix proteins, such as collagen or von Willebrand factor (vWF), often under flow conditions that mimic the circulation. Following adhesion, functional platelets spread on the surface, extending filopodia (thin, finger-like projections) and lamellipodia (broad, sheet-like extensions), which can be visualized and quantified by microscopy [ 23 , 60 ]. Activation assays provide a hallmark assessment of platelet function by enabling evaluation of rapid responses to physiological agonists such as thrombin, ADP, or collagen. This activation state is assessed via flow cytometry to measure the surface expression of specific activation markers. The most common markers are P-selectin (CD62P), which translocates from internal α-granules to the cell surface upon activation, and conformationally activated αIIbβ3 integrin, which is recognized by a PAC-1 antibody and is required for platelet aggregation [ 61 ]. Aggregation assays are used to evaluate the ability of platelets to clump together and form a hemostatic plug. The gold standard for assessing platelet function is light transmission aggregometry (LTA), in which platelet-rich plasma is stirred with an agonist in a cuvette and the increase in light transmission is recorded by a dedicated aggregometer [ 62 ]. To address the large sample volumes and technical complexity of conventional LTA, high-throughput microplate formats have been developed, in which platelet suspensions are mixed with agonists in microplate wells and turbidity changes are measured with a plate reader [ 63 ]. Secretion assays quantify the release of dense and alpha granule contents from activated platelets, including pro-aggregatory molecules such as ATP and ADP, as well as vWF, PF4, and βTG. Secretion can be quantified using a highly sensitive luciferin–luciferase bioluminescence assay or by ELISA. Luciferase uses the released ATP to generate light, where the resulting luminescence is directly proportional to the extent of dense granule secretion [ 64 , 65 ]. Clot retraction assays assess the final step in blood clot stabilization, during which the platelet cytoskeleton contracts the fibrin meshwork, compacting the clot and pulling the edges of the wound together. This function is assessed by forming a clot with platelets produced in plasma and quantifying clot retraction over time [ 11 ]. Despite their utility, these in vitro assays have limitations in evaluating manufactured platelet performance. Results can vary with the assay setup, including the surface or agonist used, shear and mixing conditions, platelet concentration, and sample handling, which can introduce pre-activation or receptor changes. No single assay should serve as a stand-alone indicator of clinical efficacy or safety, and cross-platform comparisons require standardized protocols and appropriate reference controls. These functional tests should be interpreted as a panel of complementary readouts that together define a reproducible functional profile. In vivo preclinical validation in animal models Although in vitro assays are essential for process development, more definitive proof of function and safety comes from preclinical studies in living organisms. The development of humanized models addresses an important challenge in testing human platelets, namely the species-specificity of certain receptors and ligands. For example, mouse platelets do not utilize protease-activated receptor 1, a key thrombin receptor on human platelets, for thrombin-mediated activation and differ in their interactions with factors such as vWF [ 66 ], limiting the predictive value of animal models. To overcome these limitations, humanized mouse models were developed using severely immunodeficient strains (e.g., nonobese diabetic/severe combined immune deficiency [NOD/SCID] or NOD/SCID-gamma [NSG] mice), which lack a functional immune system and can therefore accept xenogeneic human cells without rejection [ 67 ]. Adoptive transfer models provide the standard approach for testing the hemostatic function of human platelets. Recipient immunodeficient mice are made thrombocytopenic by infusing an antibody (e.g., anti-mouse GPIbα) that depletes their endogenous platelets [ 67 ]. Then, ex vivo -produced human platelets are transfused into these platelet-depleted mice, rendering the hemostatic system of the animal temporarily reliant on the human cells [ 22 , 68 ]. Post-transfusion, circulation and hemostasis are assessed to evaluate manufactured platelet efficacy. Transfused human platelets remaining in the mouse circulation are measured at various time points to determine in vivo half-life and survival kinetics, whereas their hemostatic function is directly assessed using standardized injury models, measuring both bleeding time and total blood loss [ 69 ]. More sophisticated approaches involve high-resolution intravital microscopy to directly visualize and quantify the recruitment of fluorescently labeled human platelets to a laser-induced vascular injury site in real-time [ 70 ]. Despite their utility, these models have important limitations. Innate clearance pathways remain active even in NSG and NOD/SCID mouse strains. In NSG xenotransfusion experiments, macrophage depletion partially rescued the survival of ex vivo -generated platelets, suggesting that macrophage-mediated clearance can dominate and that platelet half-lives in murine models might underestimate platelet survival in humans [ 22 ]. Desialylation is a canonical pro-clearance signal, and hepatic lectin pathways implicated in platelet removal include the Ashwell–Morell receptor (AMR) and Kupffer cell lectins, such as C-type lectin receptor 4F and macrophage galactose lectin [ 71 , 72 ]. These issues are particularly relevant for ex vivo -produced platelets, which can differ from donor-derived platelets in glycan status, receptor expression, and shear sensitivity. In NSG mice, ex vivo -produced platelets showed evidence of pre-activation, including annexin V positivity and shedding-associated loss of GPIbα (CD42b), both associated with rapid clearance, whereas infused donor platelets were comparatively quiescent before strong agonist exposure [ 22 ]. GPIbα shedding is ADAM17-dependent, and selective ADAM17 inhibition improved GPIbα retention and enhanced the vWF responsiveness and hemostatic performance of iPSC-derived platelets, supporting receptor preservation as a means of improving circulation [ 73 ]. Because GPIbα–vWF interactions are shear-dependent, non-physiological shear during manufacturing and handling can lower activation thresholds and promote activation-linked clearance phenotypes, such as shear-enhanced P-selectin translocation [ 74 ]. Non-human primate transfusion models are largely absent in the field; thus, translation to first-in-human studies has relied on convergent evidence from murine xenotransfusion data. These findings should be interpreted cautiously, given species-specific differences in platelet receptors and clearance mechanisms, as well as the limited predictive value of murine circulation and activation readouts. Such findings should be complemented by extensive in vitro comparability testing and carefully designed preclinical studies. Emerging assessment technologies and future applications Although current methods define standards for manufactured platelet assessments, emerging advanced platelet-function testing platforms (originally developed for clinical diagnostics) could represent powerful new QC tools. As the field advances toward clinical implementation, standardizing protocols and cross-platform systematic validation studies will be essential to ensure consistent and reliable assessment of manufactured platelets. Automated hematology analyzers support high-throughput, image-assisted technologies for platelet evaluation [ 75 , 76 ], whereas clinical diagnostic adaptations (such as multiple electrode aggregometry, used to measure platelet aggregation through electrical impedance in whole blood) potentially offer advantages for manufactured platelet assessment given the lower sample volumes required and real-time measurement capability. Point-of-care (POC) viscoelastic systems, such as rotational thromboelastometry, have demonstrated clinical utility in assessing global hemostasis and guiding transfusion strategies in patients with trauma [ 77 ]. Although these tests primarily evaluate clot formation (and not platelet function per se ), their rapid, whole-blood analytical format suggests potential adaptability for real-time QC analysis in ex vivo platelet production. Building upon such POC innovations, devices such as the Platelet Function Analyzer-100/200 (PFA-100/200), VerifyNow system, and Multiplate Analyzer (currently used to monitor antiplatelet therapy and diagnose platelet disorders) might be adaptable for rapid quality assessment during production, although validation for manufactured platelets would be required. Immature platelet fraction (IPF) analysis, increasingly used in clinical hematology to assess thrombopoietic activity, might provide insights into the maturation state and production dynamics of ex vivo -produced platelets. In parallel, multi-parameter flow cytometry now enables simultaneous assessment of platelet counts, size distributions, activation markers, and functional responses, potentially enabling comprehensive quality profiling. Interpretation criteria specific to ex vivo production, however, remain to be established. Automated hematology analyzers capable of IPF analysis and focused multi-parameter flow cytometry may represent practical platforms for routine product-release testing and quality assurance in hospital settings. Multiple electrode aggregometry, viscoelastic testing, the PFA-100/200, and VerifyNow provide functional-potency readouts where available and can be validated for manufactured platelets. In hospital environments, these platforms are better positioned as adjunct tools for validation, comparability assessments, or targeted investigations than as tools for universal routine release testing. Landmark achievements include the successful generation of functional, human-scale platelets from iPSCs and initiation of the first-in-human clinical trials [ 13 , 18 ]. This progress has shifted the central question from technical feasibility to safe, reproducible, and economically viable large-scale implementation, with bioprocessing, regulatory, and cost issues emerging as the major barriers. The first-in-human clinical trials A key clinical milestone was the iPLAT1 study in Japan, wherein autologous iPSC-derived platelets manufactured from patient-specific imMKCLs were transfused into a patient with aplastic anemia and severe alloimmune PTR who lacked a compatible donor. The imMKCLs, derived from the patient’s own iPSCs, provided a consistent and well-characterized starting material for GMP-compliant manufacturing. The product underwent rigorous safety testing, including acute toxicity assessment in animals, tumorigenicity, genetic variations, and verification that all reagents were clinical grade [ 13 , 18 ]. Although no acute adverse events occurred and the primary safety endpoint was met, the transfused platelets did not produce a measurable platelet count increment. This outcome may have reflected shortened survival or sequestration of cultured platelets after transfusion, potentially related to slight activation during processing. These findings underscore that functionality encompasses both clotting ability and survival in the circulation. Comparative analysis of pioneer clinical trials Building on iPLAT1, the MEG-002 trial has advanced to an allogeneic platform using iPSCs from a donor homozygous for a common Japanese HLA haplotype, representing an important step toward scalable off-the-shelf therapy ( Table 2 ). The MEG-002 product was derived from a single immunologically compatible iPSC line, thereby avoiding patient-specific manufacturing [ 78 – 80 ]. Together, the results of these landmark trials showed that iPSC-derived platelets can be administered safely under rigorous GMP conditions, but they also revealed a persistent efficacy gap. In iPLAT1, no clinically meaningful platelet increment was detected despite the transfusion of billions of platelets, highlighting a disconnect between in vitro potency assays, preclinical animal data, and clinical performance [ 18 ]. The absence of a measurable increment was attributed to potential circulation defects and methodological challenges, including the insensitivity of clinical analyzers to large platelets and suboptimal sampling timing [ 13 ]. In contrast, the platelet count measurably increased in the MEG-002 trial, and circulating transfused iPSC-derived platelets were detected by flow cytometry [ 79 ]. These results suggest that iPLAT1 limitations were more likely cell line- or process-related than inherent to the platform. This achievement represents critical proof-of-concept for the “off-the-shelf” model, and future clinical development is intensely focused on optimizing manufacturing processes to consistently produce platelets with high in vivo recovery and a normal circulatory lifespan. The transient nature of the increment and the observation that donor-comparable platelet survival was not established highlight an important remaining efficacy gap for the MEG-002 trial. In contrast, the iPLAT1 findings illustrate practical constraints of autologous manufacturing regarding cost, labor, and production timelines. Quality, storage, safety, and regulatory pathways Functional equivalence remains unresolved because ex vivo -produced platelets often show more pre-activation, size heterogeneity, and rapid clearance than donor platelets [ 18 , 78 , 81 ]. Mouse data suggest that in vivo platelet release from megakaryocytes can better reproduce native platelet properties, indicating that further refinement of culture conditions and bioreactor design is needed [ 22 ]. From a supply perspective, ex vivo production might circumvent key logistical and inventory limitations inherent to the short shelf life of donor-dependent platelet products. This advantage is supported by cryopreservable master cell banks (e.g., imMKCLs), which enable long-term storage of source material and on-demand production [ 13 ]. Ongoing efforts to cryopreserve or lyophilize final platelet products could further extend storage (facilitating stockpiling) and reduce bacterial risk [ 47 ]. For iPSC-derived products, the major safety concern is tumorigenicity from residual undifferentiated cells, requiring highly sensitive detection methods and long-term in vivo testing [ 82 – 84 ]. A powerful inherent safety feature of platelet products is their anucleate nature, allowing gamma-irradiation before transfusion to inactivate residual contaminating nucleated cells as a final safety step while generally preserving clinically acceptable platelet quality [ 85 ]. From a regulatory perspective, ex vivo -produced platelets straddle the boundary between biologics and cell therapy products, creating challenges from preclinical development to clinical trial design and requiring stringent GMP-compliant manufacturing, including control of raw materials, equipment validation, aseptic processing, and comprehensive release criteria [ 48 , 78 , 86 ]. Release testing must confirm identity, purity, potency, sterility, and safety, although defining potency assays that reliably predict in vivo efficacy remains challenging. Additional safeguards, including strategies to remove or inactivate proliferation-related genes, might be needed, but these should complement rather than replace gamma-irradiation as a final safety measure [ 87 , 88 ]. The core intellectual property of the company includes imMKCL creation from iPSCs through the transient introduction of three genes ( c-MYC , BMI1 , and BCL-XL ), enabling the establishment of large, cryopreservable master cell banks that can be expanded and differentiated on demand, with final platelet production performed in proprietary turbulent flow bioreactors [ 89 ]. Megakaryon has advanced the clinical development of stem cell-derived platelets by conducting the first-in-human Phase 1/2 trial of its lead product MEG-002. The first patient was dosed in April 2022 to evaluate the safety and efficacy of allogeneic iPSC-derived platelets in patients with thrombocytopenia [ 79 ]. HemostOD, a Swiss company founded in 2020 and based at École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne (EPFL), employs adult HSCs as starting material [ 90 ]. HemostOD has created a standardized cell source by immortalizing HSCs and engineering them to produce HLA-low universal platelets, thereby reducing HLA-mediated immune rejection risk. HemostOD demonstrated proof-of-concept for its manufacturing process utilizing advanced microfluidic bioreactors. Its lead program targets alloimmunized patients refractory to standard transfusions, and its pipeline includes engineered platelets designed as targeted drug-delivery systems. DewCell, a Korean company established in 2021, focuses on solving the primary manufacturing bottleneck of the industry [ 91 ]. It utilizes an iPSC-based platform to generate engineered artificial platelets (en-aPLTs) from standardized production cell lines. The preclinical pipelines of DewCell include applications for transfusion and regenerative medicine in osteoarthritis [ 92 ]. Additionally, the company is developing platelet lysate as a cell culture supplement, representing a potential early revenue stream. It is pursuing manufacturing scale-up by deploying 50- and 200-L bioreactors to expand production capacity. Stellular Bio, a Massachusetts-based company originally established in 2014 as Platelet BioGenesis (PlateletBio), a spin-off from Harvard University, focuses on localized therapeutic use rather than systemic transfusion. It is developing platelet-derived regenerative biologics (PRBs) mainly for Sjögren’s syndrome-related dry eye disease [ 93 ]. AdipoSeeds, established in Japan in 2016 as a Keio University spin-off, utilizes a platform based on ASCs [ 94 ]. Its core technology involves establishing mesenchymal stem cell lines (adipose-derived mesenchymal stem cell lines [ASCLs]), which are differentiated into platelet-like cells for treating intractable skin ulcers [ 95 ] or platelet transfusion applications. This manufacturing process is designed to avoid complex gene-transfer techniques and animal-derived components. The United States prioritizes battlefield and emergency readiness, as exemplified by the Fieldable Solutions for Hemorrhage with bio-Artificial Resuscitation Products program, which invests heavily in synthetic and bio-artificial red cell and platelet surrogates. The United Kingdom has advanced one of the most clinically mature cell-based strategies through the REcovery and survival of STem cell Originated REd cells trial, targeting rare blood types and alloimmunization in chronically transfused patients. The European Union follows a consortium-driven model under Horizon Europe and the European Innovation Council, supporting both biomaterial-enabled scale-up of ex vivo platelets and bottom-up synthetic erythrocytes. Japan, paralleling Korea, has centralized its strategy around iPSC technology, with coordinated programs that couple bioreactor innovation, universal cell engineering, and commercialization to establish blood products as a strategic biomanufacturing industry. Within this international landscape, Korea has established a centralized, long-term government-led program focused on developing stem cell-derived blood components. Korea launched the Cell-based Artificial Blood Technology Development Project Group on April 1, 2023, as a national consortium to secure “blood sovereignty” and position the country as a leader in producing clinically applicable laboratory-grown blood components, primarily focusing on stem cell-derived erythrocytes and platelets. Administrative operations are supported by the Regenerative Medicine Acceleration Foundation, embedding it within the advanced regenerative medicine infrastructure of the nation. Backed by a budget of approximately 47.1 billion Korean won ( \$ 35,000,000) for the first phase (2023–2027) and following a 15-yr road map divided into three stages, the initiative aims for commercialization by 2037. This platelet-development program operates through a multidisciplinary consortium spanning academia, industry, research institutes, and hospitals, linking basic research to clinical translation. University groups optimize production methods, a national institute supports industrial scale-up, hospitals provide translational expertise, and a specialized contract research organization supports regulatory alignment. This government-orchestrated collaborative framework represents a comprehensive national strategy to overcome immense technical and financial barriers to commercializing stem cell-derived platelets. It demonstrates how national governments can play crucial roles in mitigating risks and accelerating transformative biotechnology development through coordinated multi-stakeholder partnerships and sustained long-term investment, providing a stable and safe alternative to the donor-dependent blood supply system. The shift from HSC-based approaches to iPSC platforms has enabled more scalable strategies, including immortalized MK lines and CRISPR-engineered universal donor products that address immunological incompatibility. Turbulence-based bioreactors that better mimic bone marrow physiology have improved platelet yields, whereas integrated quality assessment frameworks combining flow cytometry, functional assays, and in vivo validation have strengthened product characterization. The results of early clinical trials in Japan have shown that stem cell-derived platelets can be administered safely, while exposing gaps between in vitro and in vivo performance and accelerating commercial and national translational efforts. Significant challenges remain, particularly in reducing costs and optimizing in vivo circulation. Still, the convergence of biological insight, engineering innovation, and sustained investment suggests that ex vivo platelet production could advance transfusion medicine. Initial use will likely focus on high-value niches, such as alloimmunized patients, before broader adoption as manufacturing improves. Ultimately, this approach might complement and reduce reliance on the vulnerable donor-dependent system through on-demand production of standardized, pathogen-free, and universally compatible platelets.

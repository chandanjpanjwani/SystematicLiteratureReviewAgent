Materials and methods Data collection The primary data for this study were obtained from the Science Citation Index Expanded (SCIE) database, a major sub-database of the Web of Science (WOS) database. SCIE database contains more than 9500 influential and high-quality academic journals in 178 disciplines worldwide and is a globally recognized authoritative source of information [ 18 ] . We utilized the SCIE database for data collection using the following search query: Topic = (“Organoid$” or “Microphysiological System$” or “Organ Chip$” or “Organ* on Chip$” or “Organ*-on-a-Chip$” or “Organotypic Cell Culture$” or “Organotypic Culture$” or “Organotypic Model$”). The Topic search function in WOS is a popular and effective tool for conducting research [ 19 , 20 ] . By using well-designed search phrases, researchers can ensure that their searches are both accurate and comprehensive [ 21 , 22 ] . The time span for publications was set from 1 January 2000 to 6 December 2024. The language of publication was restricted to English. Only “Articles” were included in the analysis, and all relevant bibliometric information was downloaded on 6 December 2024 to minimize the impact of database updates. The data sources and selection criteria are summarized in Figure 1 . Figure 1.: Detailed flowchart steps of the search strategy for screening literature. Data analysis VOSviewer [ 23 ] is a widely used bibliometric analysis software that facilitates various types of quantitative studies. In this study, the analysis was carried out using VOSviewer 1.6.20. Co-authorship analyses were performed for institutions and authors, co-citation analyses were carried out for references, and co-occurrence analyses were carried out for keywords. In addition, we performed descriptive analyses for the year of publication, journal, country, institution, and author. All network and overlay maps in this study were generated using VOSviewer, employing the full counting method for constructing co-occurrence relationships. To ensure accurate representation of network structures, all maps were normalized using the LinLog/modularity approaches, which optimize clustering and layout visualization. Discussion Organoid research is a rapidly developing field with a strong international presence led by the United States in terms of research output and impact. China, Germany, and Japan also play significant roles, while the Netherlands and China excel in citation impact and research recency. This bibliometric analysis shows that the United States has the greatest number of publications, with Harvard University collaborating with 210 institutions, making it a global hub for collaboration alongside other top universities. As an important scientific research tool, organoid technology mimics the functions and properties of real organs by using three-dimensional structures derived from human stem cells. This provides valuable resources for various fields, including medical research, new drug development, regenerative medicine, and personalized therapy. In the future, the impact of organoid technology is expected to increase even further, playing a crucial role in advancing human health and medical technology. Publishing trends Our analysis of organoid research from 2000 to 2024 revealed a significant increase in the number of publications related to organoids, with most of the publications occurring in the last 5 years of the study period ( Fig. 2 ). The rapid growth of publications and cumulative citations in the field indicates a growing interest in and importance of organoid research in the scientific community. The high number of publications in journals, as well as the wide involvement of countries and regions worldwide, highlights the global impact and collaborative nature of organoid research ( Fig. 4 ). The United States dominates the field in terms of the number of publications and citations, which could be attributed to the country’s robust research infrastructure, funding, and expertise in the field. However, the Netherlands stands out as a leader in terms of the average number of citations per article, indicating a high level of influence and quality of research in the country ( Table 1 ). International cooperation The collaboration between top-performing institutions and authors has facilitated the rapid and widespread use of organoid applications in medical research. Leading universities such as Harvard, the University of Washington, the Chinese Academy of Sciences, and Johns Hopkins University act as centers of international cooperation, integrating diverse expertise and technology through interdisciplinarity to advance organoid technology ( Fig. 5 ). Cooperation between high-productivity institutions promotes academic exchange between institutions, sharing of technologies and resources, and translational cooperation on research findings, all of which contribute to accelerating the speed of scientific development while providing a broader perspective and more possibilities for organoid research on a global scale. Our country co-authorship analysis (Supplemental Digital Content Figure 1, available at: https://links.lww.com/JS9/G577 ) underscores the central role of the United States in advancing global organoid research. The United States has established collaborations with 83 countries, corresponding to a total link strength (TLS) of 4826. Among these, Europe (38 countries, TLS = 2627) and Asia (24 countries, TLS = 1611) represent the most prominent partners. The strongest bilateral ties are observed with China (TLS = 670), Germany (TLS = 489), England (TLS = 357), and Japan (TLS = 305), highlighting the pivotal position of these countries in shaping the field. Importantly, these quantitative patterns reveal two regional axes of collaboration: a United States–Europe axis that reflects longstanding research consortia and funding frameworks, and a United States–Asia axis, driven by large-scale bilateral initiatives. On the transatlantic side, United States–Europe cooperation has been facilitated by the Europe–United States Agreement for Scientific and Technological Cooperation, which supports coordinated research projects, joint training of scientists and technical experts, and the exchange of materials, infrastructures, and personnel in biomedicine and health. In contrast, United States–Asia collaborations have surged in the past decade, with China emerging as the United States’ most significant partner since 2011. This growth has been underpinned by national and bilateral programs, including China’s Thousand Talents Plan and United States initiatives such as the Scientific Cooperation Exchange Program (SCEP) and the Fulbright Project, which have substantially deepened scientific exchanges and co-publications [ 26 ] . Beyond these dominant clusters, emerging collaborations with Brazil, India, and South Africa signal the diversification of the global organoid research landscape and the gradual integration of the Global South into international research networks. These findings provide not only quantitative evidence of collaboration density but also structural explanations of the geopolitical, institutional, and funding dynamics that shape international scientific partnerships. In addition to examining the density of collaborations between the United States and its global partners, this study further assessed the differences in citation impact between the United States–Europe and United States–Asia collaboration axis (Supplemental Digital Content Table S4, available at: https://links.lww.com/JS9/G577 ). Overall, the United States–Europe axis not only produced more publications ( n = 1628) than the United States–Asia axis ( n = 1401) but also exhibited significantly higher scholarly influence. Publications arising from United States–Europe collaborations accumulated 100 957 citations, nearly 1.70 times the total citations of United States–Asia collaborations ( n = 59 264). Consistently, the United States–Europe axis also exhibited a higher average citation rate ( n = 62.01) than the United States–Asia axis ( n = 42.30). In addition, the United States–Europe axis ( n = 312) produced more top 10% most-cited publications than the United States–Asia axis ( n = 206). Taken together, these results indicate that despite the rapid expansion of United States–Asia collaborations in recent years, the scholarly impact of United States–Europe collaborations remains considerably stronger. Organoid technologies entering translational acceleration To better illustrate the translational trajectory of organoid research, we systematically analyzed global patent activities using data retrieved from the Lens database and generated Supplemental Digital Content Figure S2, available at: https://links.lww.com/JS9/G577 to display the temporal dynamics of the top ten IPC classifications. Overall, organoid-related patent activities show a pronounced acceleration after 2015, suggesting a turning point from exploratory research toward technological application. In particular, C12N5/071 (undifferentiated human, animal, or plant cells) expanded rapidly and now dominates the organoid patent landscape. This trend underscores the transition of pluripotent stem cell biology into tangible organoid platforms with implications for regenerative medicine and disease modeling. Similarly, G01N33/50 (biological material analysis) and C12M3/00 (tissue culture apparatus) demonstrate steady growth, reflecting the increasing use of organoids in high-throughput drug screening, precision diagnostics, and scalable bioreactor systems designed for clinical-grade production. A representative example that underscores the clinical translation potential of organoid technologies is the foundational patent WO 2012/168930 A2 (“Culture media for stem cells”), which discloses the now-standard Wnt/R-spondin-Noggin (WRN) expansion medium for establishing long-term, genetically stable epithelial organoids. This patented culture system forms the basis of the widely adopted “Basal/Intestinal Stem Cell Medium” originally developed by the Clevers group and has since become the core methodological framework for patient-derived organoids (PDOs) across multiple cancer types [ 11 , 24 ] . This case clearly demonstrates how key biotechnology inventions can be directly translated from fundamental research into standardized tools that advance the entire field. By contrast, patents in semiconductor-related categories such as H01L23/31, H01L23/498, and H01L21/56 exhibited strong early activity but later plateaued, suggesting that microelectronic and microfluidic integration has not yet achieved broad translation into clinical or commercial settings. Likewise, C12Q1/68 (measuring or testing processes involving nucleic acids) peaked in the early 2000s and subsequently declined, indicating that while genomic testing supported the early validation of organoids, it has become less central in recent translational advances. Together, these trajectories highlight a clear divergence: biological and engineering classifications that support organoid-based diagnostics, scalable culture systems, and therapeutic development are expanding, while categories tied to more niche or saturated technologies are stabilizing or declining. This shift indicates that organoid research has moved beyond proof-of-concept biology to become an emerging technological platform with sustained industrial and clinical potential. Such developments are particularly relevant for drug discovery pipelines and regenerative therapies, where organoid systems are increasingly positioned as intermediaries between preclinical models and human trials. Research hotspots and emerging themes The five clusters aggregated according to the keywords describe the hotspots of organoid research from the perspectives of organoid model, drug screening, novel technology, molecular mechanism, and regenerative medicine, respectively, demonstrating the broad prospect of organoid research and providing new insights for the understanding and treatment of diseases. Cluster 1. Cancer organoid models This cluster primarily focuses on the cancer organoid models, with core keywords including “cancer (1064),” “organoid culture (105),” “glioblastoma (103),” “patient-derived organoids (165),” “heterogeneity (191),” “tumor (370),” “biobank (121),” and their implications for “drug screening (155),” and therapeutic evaluation. The foundation of organoid technology was laid by advancements in stem cell biology throughout the 20th century. While the term “organoid” was formally proposed earlier, a pivotal milestone was reached in 2009 when Hans Clevers and his team successfully cultivated miniature intestinal organoids [ 11 ] derived from a single Lgr5 + adult stem cell in mice for the first time. This breakthrough demonstrated the remarkable self-organization potential of stem cells [ 24 ] . Combined with the standardized culture substrate known as “matrigel,” it formed the core methodology of modern organoid technology, marking the beginning of its foundational period. Shortly thereafter, Knoblich’s team [ 16 ] published a groundbreaking paper in Nature, establishing the first method for cultivating three-dimensional brain organoids from human pluripotent stem cells. This advancement expanded the application scope of organoids in simulating complex organ development and disease research, laying the experimental foundation for the formation of related research clusters. As core methodologies matured, organoid technology entered an application and expansion phase, most notably driven by its adoption in cancer research. Applying organoid technology to cancer models represents one of the most promising areas in contemporary biomedical research, significantly advancing personalized medicine and cancer biology studies. With the development of organoid culture technology, researchers have successfully cultured many cancer organoids, including rectal [ 27 ] , gastric [ 28 , 29 ] , pancreatic [ 30 , 31 ] , breast [ 32 , 33 ] , and glioblastoma [ 34 , 35 ] . By using tumor cells from specific patients to culture organoids, researchers can study individual differences in cancer in greater detail, offering new perspectives for preclinical models of human diseases and personalized treatment strategies. Critically, cancer organoids offer distinct advantages over traditional preclinical models. Unlike conventional 2D cell lines, PDOs retain the spatial architecture, cellular heterogeneity, and genetic landscape of the original tumor. Compared to patient-derived xenograft (PDX) models in mice, organoids can be established more efficiently and at a lower cost, allowing for more scalable and high-throughput drug screening and functional genomics. A key breakthrough was the creation of large-scale living organoid biobanks [ 32 , 36–38 ] around 2015, directly addressing the bottlenecks in scale and quantity faced by traditional models. These biobanks, which encompass both healthy and tumor tissues, capture the genetic and phenotypic diversity of patient populations, enabling systematic studies of tumor biology and drug response across a multitude of genomic backgrounds. By culturing organoids from individual patients, researchers can delve into the molecular basis of tumor heterogeneity. This advances drug screening from basic research toward preclinical drug sensitivity testing and personalized therapeutic strategies, significantly bridging the gap between fundamental cancer biology and clinical application. We are currently in an era of increasing model complexity and precision, driven by cross-disciplinary integration with a range of emerging technologies. Researchers are integrating organoids with microfluidic organ-on-a-chip platforms [ 39 ] , CRISPR gene editing [ 40 ] , and single-cell sequencing technologies [ 41 ] . This convergence is propelling organoid models toward highly biomimetic miniature organs, significantly expanding their application boundaries in disease modeling and drug discovery. Cluster 2: Drug screening This cluster delineates a thematic focus on leveraging organoid systems as in vitro models for preclinical drug discovery and drug screening. This cluster is strongly associated with keywords such as “organoid (4329),” “models (1750),” “drug discovery (178),” “drug discovery (155),” “toxicity (171),” “microfluidics (425),” “platform (270),” “blood-brain barrier (110),” and “permeability (175).” Currently, the cost of new drug development is high, the clinical translation rate is low, and conventional cell lines or animal models have certain limitations [ 42 ] , while the high degree of tumor heterogeneity also makes it difficult for animal models to accurately simulate human development and disease. Patient-derived tumor-like organs retain the morphological and genetic properties as well as typical biomarker expression of the original tumor tissues and preserve tumor heterogeneity. They enable stable passage and demonstrate superior drug responsiveness compared to 2D cell models and animal models, making them ideal disease-specific models for high-throughput drug screening. Drugs that can be screened using organoids include chemotherapeutic drugs, small-molecule targeted drugs, and antibody drugs. Clevers’ team established a model of nonalcoholic fatty liver disease [ 43 ] , screened for effective drug candidates for steatosis, and revealed the molecular mechanisms by which drugs inhibit lipogenesis. Herpers et al [ 44 ] conducted a high-throughput screen of dual-targeted bispecific antibodies on organoid biobanks derived from colorectal cancer patients and paired healthy colonic mucosal samples, identifying a bispecific antibody, called MCLA-158, that blocks the developmental and survival pathways of cancer cells by degrading EGFR proteins in cancer stem cells bearing the LGR5 marker. Pellegrini et al [ 45 ] established choroid plexus-like organs that secrete cerebrospinal fluid and mimic the human blood-brain barrier to qualitatively and quantitatively predict the permeability of new drugs and selectively regulate the entry of small molecules such as dopamine and levodopa, which can be used as a screening platform for evaluating predicted human blood-cerebrospinal fluid drug permeability. The use of various organoids [ 46–49 ] to test potential drug candidates allows for more accurate prediction of drug effects in humans, thus increasing the efficiency of drug development. In 2022, the FDA approved its first drug based on research data from organ-on-a-chip technology, marking regulatory acceptance of such organoid-based platforms as drug evaluation tools. As preclinical models, organoids are also promising for drug toxicity testing and efficacy evaluation [ 50 ] , providing valuable references for late-stage clinical decision-making. Cluster 3: Novel technologies Cluster 3 delineates a thematic focus on the integration of organoids with advanced technologies to enhance their structural complexity, physiological relevance, and application scope in disease modeling and development. The discussion emphasizes key terms such as “microfluidics (425),” “chips (187),” “coculture (99),” “gene therapy (86),” and “disease modeling (88),” underscoring ongoing efforts to enhance organoid physiological relevance, engineering precision, and clinical applicability. Organoids can be combined with a variety of technologies to improve their functionality and range of applications. The combination of organoids and bioprinting technology allows for the precise control of organoid architecture and the creation of tissues with mechanical properties specific to the microenvironment [ 51 ] . The combination of organoids and microfluidics [ 52 ] provides precise control of the organoid culture environment, including pH, oxygen, and nutrient supply, and waste removal. This technology also facilitates the engineering of vascularized organoids by guiding fluid dynamics to mimic the curvature and branching of natural blood vessels, thereby improving physiological relevance and supporting the study of vascular-related diseases. Choi et al [ 53 ] demonstrated the utility of microfluidic cancer organoids by using patient-derived microfluidic cancer organoids from pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma to test several chemotherapeutic treatments, including glycogen synthase kinase inhibitors. Organ chips simulate near-physiological tissue microenvironments by integrating the advantages of organ-like and microchip technologies [ 54 ] , guiding stem cell behaviors and organ-like morphogenesis, and constructing multiorgan chips or even human body chips represents a long-term goal of this field. The liver islet-like organ chip mimics the human liver-islet axis and its glucose-stimulated response under physiological and pathological conditions [ 55 ] ; notably, the coculture system promotes robust glucose-stimulated pancreatic islet secretion in pancreatic islet-like organs and increases glucose utilization in liver-like organs as demonstrated through glucose tolerance tests. Combining microfluidic technology with organoid chips [ 56 , 57 ] can provide a controlled biomimetic microenvironment by mimicking blood circulation and intercellular substance exchange in vivo . Rifes et al [ 58 ] developed a microfluidic chip that generates different gradients of Wnt signaling proteins to guide the differentiation of human embryonic stem cells (hESCs) to differentiate on it, thus mimicking the process of early neural tube development. Gene editing tools like CRISPR-Cas9 further expand the potential of organoids for disease modeling and therapy development. Deng et al [ 59 ] , for instance, used gene editing to repair disease-causing genes in iPSCs from patients with early-onset RP, and retinal cells obtained by differentiation revealed the phenotype of healthy retinal cells. During in vitro construction, synthetic biology [ 60 , 61 ] is integrally merged with organoids to engineer cellular communities endowed with distinct functions, propelling the model’s evolution toward increased structural and functional intricacy. The strategic modulation of cellular communication, achieved through the deployment of optogenetic interfaces, distal and proximal signaling cues, and purpose-built cell adhesion mechanisms, is pivotal in orchestrating and refining the architecture of organoids. Cluster 4: Molecular mechanism Cluster 4 highlights research on the molecular mechanisms of diseases, with core keywords including “activation (933),” “disease (746),” “mechanisms (711),” and “intestinal organoids (187),” reflecting how organoid models are leveraged to dissect cellular processes, inflammatory pathways, and therapeutic vulnerabilities. As disease models, organoids enable researchers to elucidate disease activation mechanisms at the molecular and cellular levels, helping to study disease intervention and treatment better. For example, signaling pathways such as the WNT/β-catenin and MAPK pathway [ 62 ] , which are frequently dysregulated in cancer, can be directly interrogated using organoid models to validate the dynamic interactions within and between core signaling pathways in maintaining tumor growth and initiating malignant processes. These models reveal how complex molecular networks drive tumorigenesis through kinase activation, phosphorylation events, and changes in receptor-ligand binding. Studies confirm that APC gene mutations in colorectal cancer organoid models lead to sustained activation of the WNT signaling pathway, resulting in elevated expression of downstream target genes such as LGR5 [ 11 ] . Furthermore, organoid cultures have played a crucial role in elucidating the roles of key molecules like p53, E-cadherin, and TGF-β in tumor progression, invasion, and metastasis. They also facilitate investigations into DNA damage response, epigenetic modifications (e.g., methylation patterns), and the emergence of senescent or resistant phenotypes, informing the development of therapeutic strategies. The utility of organoids extends beyond cancer research into the field of human development. Single-cell sequencing studies have confirmed that gene expression programs in cerebral organoids closely mirror those in the developing human fetal neocortex [ 63 ] . By integrating organoid culture, single-cell sequencing, and gene editing technologies, researchers have identified the pivotal role of GLI3 in telencephalon morphogenesis and elucidated the gene regulatory network critical for brain development [ 64 ] . Crucially, organoid models directly bridge mechanistic discovery and clinical application. Álvarez-Varela et al [ 65 ] used rectal cancer organoids to reveal the molecular mechanisms underlying chemotherapy recurrence susceptibility by targeting the MEX3A+–resistant cell population to improve the treatment of advanced colorectal cancer. Twenty-five neuroendocrine tumor-like organ lines of gastroenteropancreatic tumors were established and comprehensively molecularly characterized by Kawasaki et al [ 66 ] Combined knockdown of TP53 and RB1, as well as overexpression of key transcription factors, provided a genetic understanding of GEP-NEN and linked these alterations to its biological phenotype. In summary, by offering a patient-specific, physiologically relevant platform, organoid technology illuminates the intricate mechanisms of diseases, from aberrant signaling in cancer to altered genetic programs in development, thereby paving the way for precision medicine. Cluster 5: Regenerative medicine Cluster 5 focuses on the application of organoid technology in regenerative medicine, with core keywords including “stem cells (2190),” “differentiation (1910),” “mouse (635),” “regeneration (460),” “colon (359),” and “repair (174),” reflecting the potential of organoid-based strategies to restore tissue and organ function through cellular and bioengineering innovations. Regenerative medicine, as an emerging field of health sciences research, aims to repair damaged tissues and organs using functional substitutes, ideally without immunosuppression and with minimal complications or toxicity. Organoids derived from adult stem cells hold particular promise in this domain due to their lower tumorigenicity and immune rejection risks compared to other transplant types. Furthermore, their capacity for large-scale expansion under controlled culture conditions makes them highly suitable for clinical applications. Multiple studies [ 67 , 68 ] have confirmed the broad potential of organoid regeneration strategies for treating various diseases. The intestinal organoid model has a mature system and was first used in regenerative medicine. Avansino et al [ 69 ] transplanted murine small intestinal adult stem cell-derived organoids were transplanted into the jejunum of recipient mice, demonstrating that the organoids were able to grow new small intestinal mucosa in the transplanted area and differentiate into intestinal epithelial cells and goblet cells. In the highly regarded field of liver regeneration, patient-specific liver organoids hold promise for overcoming critical bottlenecks of donor shortage and immune rejection. Current liver transplantation faces immune-related challenges, while scalable production of human liver organoids offers a potential solution. Hu et al [ 70 ] transplanted human fetal liver-like organs into a mouse model of liver injury, and the results demonstrated that liver-like organs can significantly expand and regenerate damaged liver tissue after transplantation. The integration of organoids with gene editing and bioengineering technologies further expands their therapeutic potential. Clevers et al [ 71 ] used CRISPR-dependent single-base editing to repair mutations in the CFTR causative gene in cystic fibrosis patient samples from the Dutch Organoid Biobank, providing a clinically matched therapeutic option for cystic fibrosis disease. Wu et al [ 72 ] established a multifunctional blastocyst complementation platform based on CRISPR-Cas9-mediated genome editing of fertilized eggs. The rat pancreas was grown in vivo in mice. The rat pancreas was grown in vivo in mice, successfully demonstrating interspecies blastocyst complementation in pancreas-knockout mice. Looking forward, personalized stem cell-derived organoids may help overcome the immune rejection of artificial organ transplants, and the deep integration of micro- and nanoengineering–based organ maturation and personalized organoid transplantation is expected to advance regenerative organ therapies. Nanotechnology-constructed biomaterial scaffolds provide an environment conducive to cell adhesion, differentiation, and proliferation for the self-organization of organoid pluripotent stem cells [ 73 ] . In addition, microfluidic technology controls the differentiation of stem cells to repair nervous system damage, providing new opportunities for regenerative medicine. Together, these converging technologies are paving the way for organoid-based regenerative therapies. Current challenges and remaining limitations in organoid research However, alongside these remarkable advances, the field confronts significant challenges and limitations that must be addressed to fully realize its translational potential. A primary biological constraint is the frequent absence of key microenvironmental components, such as functional vasculature and immune cells, which restricts nutrient diffusion, limits long-term culture, and hampers the modeling of systemic interactions. Technically, the reliance on ill-defined matrices like Matrigel and a lack of standardized protocols impede reproducibility and scalability, posing barriers to clinical adoption. In oncology, patient-derived tumor organoids may fail to capture the full heterogeneity of the original malignancy, potentially biasing drug-response predictions. Furthermore, the rapid progression of brain organoids has sparked ethical debates concerning the emergence of neural complexity and consciousness. Addressing these limitations – through engineered vascularization, immune integration, defined culture systems, and ethical frameworks – is crucial for transforming organoids from a powerful research model into a robust platform for personalized medicine and regenerative therapies. Limitations As with other bibliometric studies, this paper has several limitations. Only English-language publications were selected, and there may be a language bias. Furthermore, there are differences in authors’ and institutions’ attributions, which may cause bias in the statistical results. Finally, given the timing of the retrieval, the 2024 data remain incomplete. Conclusion Organoid-related research witnessed a period of exponential growth between 2000 and 2024, a phenomenon indicative of the enduring interest of scholars and the transformative potential of this research area. The United States, with institutions such as Harvard University at the forefront, is the foremost contributor in terms of both output and impact. The field is propelled forward by influential journals such as Cell and leading authors such as Hans Clevers. Notable research areas include cancer organoid models, drug screening, innovative technical and molecular mechanisms, and regenerative medicine. This study emphasizes the advancements and global collaborations in organoids, underscoring their role as a catalyst for biomedical research. Organoids are considered as a pivotal tool for enhancing the comprehension of complex diseases, thereby providing unparalleled avenues for scientific discovery. Ethical approval Not applicable.

However, traditional methods of bioprospecting are time-consuming, resource-intensive, and limited by the complexity of natural compounds and ecosystems. Artificial Intelligence (AI) is emerging as a transformative tool in this field, significantly enhancing the speed, efficiency, and accuracy of natural compound discovery. This review explores the application of AI in bioprospecting for bioactive natural products, particularly for biopharmaceutical purposes. We discuss the current AI techniques employed in bioprospecting, such as machine learning (ML), deep learning (DL), and cheminformatics, and how they are used to predict bioactivity, screen large compound libraries, and design novel bioactive molecules. AI-driven tools enable the exploration of natural products from diverse and previously inaccessible ecosystems, while also assisting in optimizing high-throughput screening processes. Furthermore, we evaluate case studies where AI has successfully identified antimicrobial, anticancer, and anti-inflammatory compounds from natural sources. However, integrating AI in bioprospecting also presents challenges, including data quality issues, model interpretability, and ethical concerns related to biodiversity conservation. Looking ahead, we explore emerging trends in AI-driven bioprospecting, including its potential to revolutionize personalized medicine and enable precision bioprospecting. We conclude by emphasizing the critical role of cross-disciplinary collaboration in leveraging AI’s capabilities to drive sustainable and efficient biopharmaceutical development, paving the way for a new era in natural product drug discovery. Biological Techniques Compound Screening Computational Intelligence Natural Product Synthesis Artificial Intelligence Bioprocess Engineering Ethnopharmacology and Natural Products in Drug Discovery Introduction Bioprospecting refers to the systematic search for bioactive compounds from natural sources such as plants, fungi, bacteria, and marine organisms. This process has historically played a pivotal role in the discovery of new drugs, with many therapeutics derived from or inspired by natural products. Notable examples include antibiotics such as penicillin, anticancer agents like paclitaxel (Taxol), and immunosuppressants such as cyclosporine [ 1 ]. Natural products possess remarkable chemical diversity and structural complexity, often surpassing synthetic chemical libraries in terms of diversity and biological relevance [ 2 ]. Despite the potential of natural compounds, traditional bioprospecting methods—comprising extraction, isolation, and bioactivity screening—are often time-consuming and labor-intensive. These techniques are also constrained by the vast chemical space of natural products and the complexity of ecosystems, which can limit the efficiency of drug discovery. Additionally, biodiversity loss and the overexploitation of natural resources have led to the depletion of some valuable sources of bioactive compounds [ 3 ]. As bioprospecting continues to evolve, integrating modern technologies becomes essential to overcome these challenges. Challenges in traditional bioprospecting In traditional bioprospecting, natural samples are collected from various ecosystems, followed by the extraction of chemical compounds and bioassays to test for potential therapeutic properties (see Fig. 1 ). While effective in some instances, such as the discovery of antibiotics from soil microbes, this method often results in low hit rates due to the complexity of natural extracts and the challenges in isolating active compounds from mixtures. Moreover, natural products frequently exhibit complex structures that are difficult to synthesize or modify chemically [ 4 ]. Several obstacles hinder the efficiency of traditional bioprospecting: High chemical complexity : Natural products exhibit high stereochemistry and diverse molecular frameworks, making them difficult to analyze and optimize for drug development. Slow screening processes : The extraction and bioassay process are laborious, requiring significant resources and time to identify a few bioactive leads. Biodiversity loss : Habitat destruction and unsustainable harvesting practices threaten ecosystems that may harbor valuable natural products. Limited accessibility : Some natural environments, such as deep-sea or high-altitude ecosystems, remain underexplored due to their remote and challenging conditions. Full size image Enhancing traditional Bioprospecting through AI integration The role of AI in modern bioprospecting With the rise of Artificial Intelligence (AI), a branch of computer science that uses computational models to perform tasks that previously required human intelligence, bioprospecting has entered a new era. AI, through machine learning (ML), deep learning (DL), and other data-driven techniques, has the potential to revolutionize the search for bioactive natural compounds. AI can handle large, complex datasets, making it possible to explore vast chemical spaces more efficiently than traditional methods. For example, ML algorithms can analyze the structure-activity relationship (SAR) of natural compounds, predicting their potential bioactivity based on their chemical structure [ 5 ]. AI also accelerates the high-throughput screening (HTS) process by automating and optimizing data analysis, significantly reducing the time and cost of identifying drug candidates [ 6 , 7 ]. Additionally, AI-driven tools can integrate data from various fields—such as genomics, metabolomics, and cheminformatics—to predict bioactivity, identify biosynthetic gene clusters, and discover novel compounds from previously untapped natural sources [ 8 ]. This review explores the current and potential applications of AI in bioprospecting for bioactive compounds, focusing on the development of natural products for biopharmaceutical purposes. We will discuss the various AI techniques used in bioprospecting, the challenges of integrating AI into natural product discovery, and future opportunities for AI-driven bioprospecting. By highlighting recent research trends and case studies, this review aims to provide a comprehensive understanding of how AI is reshaping the field of natural product drug discovery. AI techniques in bioprospecting Machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) in natural compound discovery Machine‑learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) techniques are now central to natural‑product bioprospecting, where they complement traditional bioassays by extracting predictive rules from large genomic, metabolomic, and cheminformatics datasets [ 9 , 10 , 11 , 12 ]. ML develops algorithms that learn from data and then generalize to unseen cases [ 13 ]. Support‑vector machines illustrate this principle by fitting an optimal hyperplane that separates classes; they have been used to correlate chemical descriptors of plant metabolites with antiproliferative activity, enabling early identification of promising anticancer scaffolds [ 14 ]. Random forests, which aggregate predictions from many decision trees, excel at structure–activity‑relationship analysis and routinely rank molecular substructures by their contribution to bioactivity. This capability has improved hit rates in several natural‑product screens [ 15 ]. Artificial neural networks push pattern‑recognition capacity further: deep multilayer models trained on millions of chemical–activity pairs recently uncovered the broad‑spectrum antibiotic halicin, demonstrating how network‑based learning can reveal new antimicrobial chemotypes in vast compound libraries [ 16 ]. By combining these algorithms with curated structural and bioactivity databases, researchers can predict the therapeutic potential of untested natural compounds, focus wet‑lab resources on the highest‑value candidates, and shorten discovery timelines that once depended on exhaustive high‑throughput screening. Deep Learning (DL): unlocking complex biological patterns Deep learning, a subset of ML, employs deep neural networks (DNNs) with multiple layers to process complex datasets. Unlike traditional ML, DL algorithms can automatically extract meaningful features from raw data without manual engineering, making them highly versatile in analyzing high-dimensional datasets like genomics, transcriptomics, and metabolomics. Applications of DL in Natural Compound Discovery: 1. Molecular Property Prediction: Deep learning algorithms such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been applied to predict molecular properties, including solubility, permeability, and toxicity [ 17 , 18 ]. 2. Virtual Screening: DL-powered virtual screening is a pivotal application in bioprospecting. These methods use compound libraries to predict the binding affinity of natural products to specific biological targets, facilitating lead identification. A unified approach combining recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and CNNs, for instance, has been used for sequence-based predictions of compound-protein interactions [ 19 ]. 3. De Novo Molecular Design: Generative adversarial networks (GANs) and autoencoders are DL architectures that can design novel compounds by learning from the structural diversity of existing natural products. These tools expand the chemical space for drug discovery, allowing the generation of entirely new molecules with desired properties [ 20 ]. 4. Large-Scale Omics Integration: DL can process and integrate multi-omics datasets (e.g., genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics) to identify bioactive metabolites [ 21 ]. For instance, DL models were used to identify biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) responsible for producing novel secondary metabolites in microbes, enhancing natural product discovery [ 22 ]. Advantages of ML and DL in Bioprospecting. Both ML and DL offer advantages over traditional methods. The following are the advantages - Efficiency: Algorithms can analyze millions of compounds in a fraction of the time needed for traditional screening. Accuracy: By leveraging large datasets, these methods improve predictive accuracy, minimizing false positives and negatives. Scalability: ML/DL models can be easily scaled to accommodate increasing data sizes, enabling high-throughput analysis [ 23 ]. Cheminformatics integrating AI Cheminformatics, as a computational field, relies heavily on extracting, analyzing, and predicting chemical data. The integration of artificial intelligence (AI), machine learning (ML), and deep learning (DL) has brought unprecedented levels of accuracy and efficiency to cheminformatics workflows, especially concerning feature extraction, prediction, and compound design [ 24 ]. AI integrates into cheminformatics primarily through chemical feature extraction (Mitchell, 2014). Many ML techniques analyze molecular descriptors—quantitative representations of chemical structures capturing attributes such as molecular weight, hydrophobicity, or electronegativity [ 25 ]. Algorithms such as random forests (RF), support vector machines (SVM), and gradient boosting machines (GBM) are commonly used to map these descriptors to properties like bioactivity, toxicity, and solubility [ 26 ]. For example, a random forest model might be trained on a dataset of known bioactive molecules to predict whether new compounds will exhibit similar activity [ 27 ]. Feature extraction also benefits from deep learning methods, such as deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs), which can learn spatial patterns directly from 2D molecular graphs or images of molecular structures [ 28 ]. AI is also extensively used in virtual screening, where chemical libraries containing millions of potential compounds are evaluated for their likelihood of binding to a biological target [ 7 ]. Traditional virtual screening methods are computationally expensive, but ML and DL models drastically reduce this burden. For example, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and transformer-based architectures are increasingly employed in cheminformatics to process SMILES (Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System) strings—text representations of chemical structures—to predict chemical properties or activities [ 29 ]. In drug discovery pipelines, these methods allow researchers to prioritize compounds for synthesis or testing based on their predicted efficacy and safety [ 20 ]. Another core area where AI and cheminformatics intersect is in molecular docking and structure-based drug design. Molecular docking simulations, which predict how a small molecule binds to a target protein, are computationally intensive, requiring detailed modeling of molecular interactions. AI can accelerate this process by training models on existing docking data to predict binding affinities more quickly. For instance, graph neural networks (GNNs) have been applied to represent molecules and proteins as graphs, where nodes represent atoms and edges represent bonds or interactions. These models learn to predict binding affinity based on the topology of the molecular and target protein graphs, bypassing the need for exhaustive molecular dynamics simulations [ 30 ]. In de novo compound design, DL models like variational autoencoders (VAEs) and generative adversarial networks (GANs) are used to design new molecules with specific properties. These models learn from existing libraries of chemical structures and generate novel compounds that satisfy predefined constraints, such as high bioactivity or low toxicity. Reinforcement learning (RL) is also paired with generative models to optimize compound design iteratively, allowing researchers to explore chemical space in a goal-directed manner [ 31 ]. Overall, AI/ML/DL methods in cheminformatics provide a powerful toolkit for analyzing chemical data, accelerating virtual screening, improving predictive modeling, and enabling the design of innovative compounds that would otherwise remain unexplored [ 26 ]. The application of AI/ML/DL in cheminformatics has led to significant breakthroughs in drug discovery and chemical analysis. Below are case-study-like examples that illustrate the use of these techniques: 1. Machine Learning-Based virtual screening for antiviral compounds A study by Samad et al. [ 32 ] demonstrated how ML-based virtual screening, in combination with MD simulation, was used to predict the bioactivity of natural products against SARS-CoV-2. The researchers employed random forests to analyze the molecular descriptors of thousands of phytochemical compounds from the literature. Through this effort, they predicted which compounds were likely to exhibit bioactivity by inhibiting a critical protease involved in the replication of the virus. 2. Deep learning for molecular docking A notable study by Ragoza and coworkers [ 33 ] Introduced a convolutional neural network (CNN) - based model outperforming traditional scoring functions in molecular docking simulations. The model was trained on protein-ligand complexes from the PDBBind database, using 3D voxel representations of molecules as input. The CNN learned spatial features from these complexes, enabling it to predict binding affinities with remarkable accuracy. For example, in virtual screening for inhibitors of a kinase target, the model identified multiple lead compounds that traditional docking tools missed, later validated by in vitro assays. 3. Generative models for de Novo drug design Popova et al. [ 34 ] illustrates a groundbreaking application of generative models. They used a combination of Generative Neural Networks (GNN) and Reinforcement learning (RL) to design novel chemical structures optimized for specific properties, such as drug-likeness and binding affinity. By training on SMILES strings of known drugs, the model learned patterns in chemical structures and created entirely new molecules that adhered to desired constraints. In one application, the RNN generated a novel compound with high predicted affinity for Janus protein kinase 2. 4. Graph neural networks (GNNs) for predicting Protein-Ligand binding Nguyen et al. [ 35 ] developed GraphDTA, a messagepassing GNN that represents drug molecules as graphs and predicts binding affinity to protein targets directly from sequence and structural information. Their model outperformed traditional fingerprintbased methods on benchmark datasets (KIBA and Davis), demonstrating the practical value of graph neural networks for ranking drug candidates. Metabolomics integrating AI Metabolomics generates complex, high-dimensional datasets that capture the concentrations of metabolites under different biological conditions. Integrating AI, ML, and DL has revolutionized metabolomics, allowing researchers to analyze this data more effectively and extract biologically meaningful insights. AI’s role in metabolomics spans from data preprocessing and feature extraction to biomarker discovery and pathway analysis [ 36 , 37 ]. Data preprocessing - is a crucial step in metabolomics, given the high variability and noise inherent in the data. Techniques like principal component analysis (PCA), independent component analysis (ICA ) , and t-SNE (t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding) are often applied for dimensionality reduction, simplifying complex datasets while retaining the most relevant features [ 11 ]. These methods are commonly paired with unsupervised ML clustering algorithms, such as k-means clustering, hierarchical clustering, or density-based spatial clustering of applications with noise (DBSCAN), to group samples based on metabolite profiles and identify underlying patterns [ 38 ]. AI also plays a significant role in metabolite feature extraction. Many ML algorithms, including support vector regression (SVR), decision trees, and extreme gradient boosting (XGBoost), are used to predict metabolite properties or associations. DL methods like CNNs are particularly effective in metabolomics when applied to spectral data from techniques like mass spectrometry (MS) or nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) [ 39 , 40 ]. For example, CNNs can learn features directly from MS spectra, distinguishing between metabolites based on subtle differences in their spectral patterns. This eliminates the need for manual feature engineering and increases accuracy in metabolite identification [ 39 ]. One of the most transformative applications of AI in metabolomics is biomarker discovery, where algorithms identify metabolites that differentiate between healthy and diseased states. Supervised ML algorithms such as random forests, SVMs, and logistic regression are widely used to identify discriminative features from labeled metabolomic datasets. For example, Troisi et al. [ 41 ] investigated the application of metabolomics, with a specific focus on plasma metabolites, for the early detection of colorectal cancer (CRC). They employed an ensemble model to identify a signature of metabolites that could be used to distinguish between individuals with colorectal cancer (CRC) and those who are healthy. The research found that a combination of specific metabolites involved in sugar metabolism showed promise in identifying CRC, potentially offering a non-invasive screening tool. Another exciting area is the integration of metabolomics with other omics data. ML algorithms like multi-omics integration models combine metabolomics data with genomics, transcriptomics, or proteomics data to provide a comprehensive view of biological systems. For example, ML models can link altered metabolite levels with genetic mutations, identifying metabolic pathways that may be targeted for therapeutic intervention. Bayesian networks are also commonly applied to model causal relationships between different types of omics data, helping researchers understand the interplay between metabolites and other molecular components [ 11 , 21 ]. AI is further applied to predict metabolite interactions and biological activities, such as enzyme binding or receptor activation. Similar to cheminformatics, ML models like SVMs and DL techniques like GNNs predict how metabolites interact with specific biological targets. This is particularly valuable for identifying bioactive metabolites in natural products or plant extracts, accelerating the drug discovery process [ 42 ]. The integration of AI/ML/DL into metabolomics has similarly advanced the field, particularly in biomarker discovery and metabolite identification. The following case-study-like examples provide practical applications: 1. RF and SVM for Biomarker Discovery in Cancer - A study by Anh et al. [ 43 ] applied RF and SVM techniques to metabolomics data from cancer patients to identify biomarkers for early-stage breast cancer. The dataset consisted of mass spectrometry (MS) spectra from metabolomics and lipidomics analyses of plasma samples. The models were trained to classify patients based on cancer stage and successfully identified key metabolites, such as phospholipids and hydrophilic metabolites, that were strongly associated with tumor progression. 2. CNNs for mass Spectrometry-Based metabolite identification Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have already been used for direct metabolite annotation from raw MS/MS spectra: Gao et al. [ 39 ] developed a CNN that learns spectral fingerprints and predicts molecular substructures without manual peak matching, enabling the model to rank candidate identities for untargeted LC‑MS data far more accurately than traditional algorithms. When applied to diverse biological samples, the network consistently recovered known metabolites and proposed credible identities for unknown peaks, substantially streamlining the metabolite-identification workflow and demonstrating the superiority of CNNs over conventional feature engineering approaches. 3. Random Forests for Metabolomics and Clinical Integration - A pertinent study by Wang and coworkers [ 44 ] employed random forest, alongside other models, to analyze plasma metabolomics data from a large Chinese cohort, aiming to identify biomarkers associated with metabolic syndrome (MetS), a condition closely linked to type 2 diabetes. The researchers identified 13 significantly differential metabolites, with pathway enrichment analysis revealing arginine, proline, and glutathione metabolism disturbances. The random forest model demonstrated high predictive accuracy, achieving an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.993, underscoring the efficacy of machine learning in integrating clinical and metabolomic data for biomarker discovery. 4. Autoencoders for Dimensionality Reduction and Noise Removal - A study by Wang et al. [ 45 ] employed stacked autoencoders to denoise magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS) data, enhancing the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and reducing acquisition time. Their approach demonstrated significant improvements in SNR and mean squared error (MSE) across both phantom and human brain data, suggesting that autoencoders can effectively preprocess metabolomic data, facilitating more accurate downstream analyses such as clustering and biomarker identification. While cheminformatics focuses on analyzing molecular structures and chemical properties, metabolomics deals with complex biological systems and metabolite interactions [ 46 ]. The integration of AI/ML/DL into both fields allows for complementary insights: In summary, AI, ML, and DL methods in metabolomics provide advanced solutions for data preprocessing, feature extraction, biomarker discovery, and pathway analysis [ 47 ]. By integrating these techniques, researchers can make sense of complex metabolomic data and uncover new therapeutic insights [ 36 ]. Similarly, AI/ML/DL techniques are transforming cheminformatics, from virtual screening to de novo drug design, by making predictive models faster, more accurate, and capable of handling the complexity of chemical and biological data [ 24 ]. Natural Language Processing (NLP) in bioprospecting Natural Language Processing (NLP), a branch of AI that focuses on analyzing and understanding human language, has emerged as a powerful tool for bioprospecting. Given the vast and ever-growing volume of scientific literature, NLP provides an efficient way to extract relevant information about natural products, their properties, and their potential applications in drug discovery [ 48 , 49 ]. One of the primary applications of NLP in bioprospecting is automating the extraction of data from unstructured text, such as research articles, patents, and databases. NLP algorithms are trained to recognize patterns in text, identifying information on chemical structures, biological targets, bioactivity, and toxicity [ 50 , 51 ]. Named entity recognition (NER) is a common NLP technique used to identify specific terms, such as compound names, species, or disease states, within large text corpora. Relationship extraction algorithms then link these entities, creating structured datasets that can be further analyzed to generate hypotheses about potential drug candidates [ 52 ]. As an example of practical application, NLP has been used to mine literature for information on antiviral compounds derived from natural products. By analyzing thousands of research articles, NLP models can identify promising candidates based on mentions of their bioactivity, mechanisms of action, or interactions with viral targets. This accelerates the process of hypothesis generation and compound identification, enabling researchers to focus their experimental efforts on the most promising leads [ 53 ]. NLP also facilitates the integration of text-based information with other types of data. For instance, information extracted from the literature can be combined with cheminformatics or metabolomics datasets to provide a more comprehensive understanding of natural products. Text mining tools can identify compounds with reported bioactivity, which can then be cross-referenced with cheminformatics predictions or metabolomic analyses to validate their potential as drug candidates [ 48 ]. Another emerging area of NLP in bioprospecting is the use of generative models to suggest novel hypotheses or predict underexplored areas of research. These models analyze existing literature to identify gaps in knowledge or trends that suggest new directions for experimental investigation. By leveraging the vast body of published knowledge, NLP empowers researchers to stay ahead in the rapidly evolving field of natural product discovery [ 54 ]. Typical AI/ML workflow for natural product data Data preprocessing High‑quality input is essential before any machine‑learning model can learn from natural‑product data. Typical datasets combine structural descriptors (SMILES strings, InChI keys, molecular graphs), activity measurements such as IC₅₀ or binding affinities, and high‑dimensional spectra from mass‑spectrometry or NMR analyses [ 9 , 55 ]. Pre‑processing begins by addressing missing values and obvious errors: incomplete activity records are either imputed with statistically appropriate surrogates or removed, and noisy peaks in spectral files are filtered out. Next, continuous variables that span different scales, such as molecular weight, log P, polar surface area, etc., are brought to a common range with standard or min-max scaling so that no single descriptor dominates the learning process. Finally, the data are encoded in a format that matches the chosen algorithm. Tokenized SMILES strings feed recurrent or transformer networks, molecular graphs are converted to node–edge tensors for graph neural networks, and cleaned spectral peaks become input vectors for convolutional models. These steps create a consistent, information‑rich matrix from which AI systems can extract reliable structure‑activity relationships. For a typical workflow utilizing Random Forests (RF) or SVMs to analyze natural products for bioactivity, molecular descriptors (e.g., logP, topological polar surface area, molecular weight) are computed using cheminformatics tools like RDKit. Spectral data from natural product extracts is transformed into a tabular format (e.g., peak intensities at specific m/z ratios) to ensure compatibility with traditional ML algorithms like RF or SVM. In a GNN workflow, a molecule, such as a flavonoid, is represented as a graph where nodes represent atoms and edges represent chemical bonds. The graph is input into a GNN model after preprocessing the adjacency and node feature matrices. Feature selection Feature selection is crucial when applying classical machine learning algorithms such as SVMs and RFs to high-dimensional metabolomics or cheminformatics data, as excessive and noisy variables can degrade model performance [ 56 , 57 ]. RFs provide a built-in solution by ranking variables according to their contribution to decision splits, while SVMs typically use recursive feature elimination or kernel-based relevance scores to remove uninformative descriptors. Domain expertise further aids in descriptor pruning; cheminformatics workflows generally begin with physicochemical features, including molecular weight, logP, hydrogen-bond donors/acceptors, and aromatic ring count, then eliminate highly correlated or irrelevant variables before constructing quantitative structure–activity relationship (QSAR) models. To highlight the importance of feature selection, a recent QSAR study of durian-derived anti-inflammatory compounds by Wiriyarattanakul et al. [ 58 ] found that the authors began with several hundred steric and electrostatic descriptors calculated for approximately one thousand naturally derived molecules. They ranked the variables using Random Forest feature importance scores and selected the top 50 parameters—including logP, molecular weight, and polarizability—for model building. The reduced descriptor set produced RF and gradient boosting models with external test R² values exceeding 0.80, demonstrating that RF-based feature selection reduces overfitting while enhancing interpretability and predictive power. Model training After selecting informative features, the next step is to fit predictive models that estimate outcomes such as bioactivity, toxicity, or metabolite identity. Support vector machines (SVMs) are trained by identifying a hyperplane that maximally separates classes—for example, active versus inactive compounds. Non-linear kernels, such as the radial basis function, are commonly used in cheminformatics because they capture complex structure–activity relationships, as long as the regularization parameter (C) and kernel coefficient (γ) are tuned carefully [ 59 ]. In contrast, random forest (RF) models build an ensemble of decision trees created from bootstrap samples; this algorithm naturally handles non-linear patterns and provides variable-importance scores, which are particularly helpful when ranking heterogeneous chemical descriptors in natural-product studies [ 15 , 60 ]. Deep-learning approaches extend these concepts to much higher dimensional inputs: fully connected neural networks map long descriptor vectors or even raw SMILES strings to bioactivity values [ 61 ], while convolutional architectures have been trained directly on mass-spectrometry spectra to classify metabolites with minimal preprocessing [ 62 ]. Graph neural networks (GNNs) further generalize the deep-learning paradigm by treating each molecule as a graph, in which nodes (atoms) and edges (bonds) communicate across layers, a strategy that has been shown to improve property prediction for natural-product scaffolds [ 63 , 64 ]. Cross-validation To ensure that these models generalize beyond their training data, investigators rely on cross-validation. The most common design is k-fold cross-validation, where the data are partitioned into k subsets, and each subset is held out once as a test fold; this procedure is considered best practice for SVM, RF, and most deep learning pipelines [ 65 , 66 ]. Leave-one-out cross-validation is sometimes preferred for very small natural product collections, while nested cross-validation provides an unbiased estimate of performance when hyperparameters are tuned within inner loops [ 67 ]. The importance of cross-validation is demonstrated by Cockroft et al. [ 68 ], who developed the STarFish stacked ensemble model for target prediction and benchmarked it on a curated natural product set (1,943 compounds spanning diverse chemical classes). Model performance was evaluated using stratified 10-fold cross-validation, ensuring each fold contained a balanced number of alkaloids, flavonoids, terpenes, and other scaffolds; this procedure markedly improved the reliability of both the random forest and support vector–based base learners before stacking. Interpretability issues Interpretability continues to pose a challenge for complex models. Linear-kernel SVMs are relatively transparent because their feature weights map directly onto the separating hyperplane, but non-linear kernels sacrifice interpretability for higher accuracy [ 69 ]. RFs strike a balance by providing feature-importance scores; in a typical quantitative structure–activity relationship (QSAR) study, an RF might reveal that molecular weight accounts for 35% of the variance in activity. In comparison, logP contributes 25% [ 70 ]. Deep neural networks, such as multilayer perceptrons or GNNs, are often regarded as “black boxes.” However, post-hoc explanation tools—SHAP or LIME for tabular inputs [ 71 ] and GNN Explainer for graph data [ 72 ]—can highlight the descriptors or substructures that drive individual predictions. A recent antibacterial QSAR study illustrates a complete workflow. Researchers compiled 5,000 natural products represented by SMILES strings [ 73 ] and calculated 500 physicochemical descriptors. RF importance scores reduced this set to the 50 most informative variables, which were then fed into both SVM and RF models, while a separate deep neural network was trained directly on the tokenized SMILES [ 74 ]. Ten-fold nested cross-validation confirmed robust performance across alkaloids, flavonoids, and other chemical classes. Finally, SHAP analysis pinpointed logP and aromaticity as key determinants of antibacterial activity, and GNN Explainer highlighted the heteroaromatic ring systems most strongly associated with high potency. Together, these steps—feature selection, model training, rigorous validation, and interpretability analysis—form a coherent pipeline for reliable machine-learning predictions in natural-product research. Genomics and AI: the integration of AI in genomic mining for natural products Advances in genomics have opened up significant opportunities for bioprospecting, particularly for identifying bioactive natural compounds produced by microorganisms and plants. Genomic data can reveal biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) responsible for the production of secondary metabolites—compounds that often possess therapeutic properties, such as antibiotics, anticancer agents, and immunosuppressants (see Fig. 2 ). However, the sheer volume and complexity of genomic data make it difficult to analyze manually. AI, ML, and DL are transforming the field of genomics by enabling the efficient mining of these large datasets to discover new bioactive compounds [ 8 ]. Full size image De novo versus Analog drug design process AI in biosynthetic gene cluster identification AI has shown exceptional potential in identifying BGCs within microbial genomes. Biosynthetic gene clusters are groups of co-located genes that collectively encode the enzymes necessary for the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites. These metabolites, such as polyketides, non-ribosomal peptides, and terpenes, are valuable for biopharmaceutical applications due to their bioactive properties [ 75 ]. One AI-based tool, antiSMASH (Antibiotics & Secondary Metabolite Analysis Shell), is a widely used platform for detecting BGCs in bacterial and fungal genomes. Initially developed as a bioinformatics tool, antiSMASH can now collaborate with Neural networks (NNs) and related tools that implement machine learning techniques indirectly to predict the chemical structures of the metabolites produced by these gene clusters [ 76 , 77 ]. By integrating AI, antiSMASH can rapidly and accurately identify novel BGCs, even from previously unexplored microbial species. This greatly accelerates the discovery of new natural compounds with potential pharmaceutical applications, such as novel antibiotics and anticancer agents. In addition, AI tools such as DeepBGC utilize deep learning models, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), to identify novel BGCs in microbial genomes with high accuracy [ 22 ]. DeepBGC improves on traditional methods by identifying known BGCs and predicting new, previously undiscovered gene clusters that may produce novel bioactive compounds. This represents a significant advancement, as traditional methods often miss cryptic BGCs—clusters that are not expressed under normal laboratory conditions but may produce bioactive metabolites in natural environments. AI in microbial genomics: exploring the microbiome for natural products The human microbiome and environmental microbial communities are prolific sources of natural compounds with bioactive properties. However, the vast diversity of microbial species and the complexity of their genomic content present challenges for bioprospecting. AI models, particularly those based on deep learning, are being used to analyze metagenomic data—genomic data derived from entire microbial communities [ 78 ]. This approach allows researchers to study non-culturable microbes, which constitute the majority of microbial diversity but cannot be grown in laboratory conditions. AI models can sift through metagenomic datasets to identify microbial species that harbor BGCs to produce bioactive compounds. For example, Yan et al. [ 79 ]. Introduced Seq2PKS, a deeplearning workflow that links bacterial genomes to the chemical structures of type I cisAT polyketides. After training on thousands of annotated biosynthetic gene clusters, the model sifted through marine actinobacterial genomes, predicted complete polyketide scaffolds, and flagged several previously uncharacterized clusters from Streptomyces strains. Guided by these predictions, the authors isolated and structurally confirmed three metabolites, monazomycin, oasomycin A, and 2aminobenzamideactiphenol, that showed lowmicromolar cytotoxicity against a panel of human tumor cell lines. The work demonstrates how machinelearningbased genome mining can uncover new anticancer agents from marine “microbial dark matter.” AI and plant genomics: discovering bioactive compounds from medicinal plants Plants have long served as a rich source of bioactive compounds, including alkaloids, flavonoids, and terpenoids, which are widely utilized in the pharmaceutical industry. AI is increasingly being applied to plant genomics to uncover the genetic basis for the biosynthesis of these compounds and to accelerate the discovery of new plant-derived drugs. AI-driven genomic mining has proven particularly beneficial in identifying BGCs in plant genomes that encode enzymes responsible for producing secondary metabolites with medicinal properties. For instance, a recent study by Bai and coworkers [ 80 ] used machine learning to predict genes responsible for the biosynthesis of plant specialized metabolites by integrating multi-omics data. While their research focused on Arabidopsis, the methodologies developed may be applicable to other plant species. By analyzing the expression profiles of genes associated with secondary metabolite biosynthesis, the AI model was able to predict new pathways that could be targeted to increase artemisinin production or to discover new compounds with similar bioactivity. Another application of AI in plant genomics is using neural networks to predict how specific environmental conditions (such as light, temperature, and soil nutrients) influence the expression of BGCs and the production of bioactive compounds in medicinal plants. A recent study by Saffariha and coworkers [ 81 ] built multilayer perceptron and radial basis function neural network models to forecast the content of hypericin in Hypericum perforatum grown under varying light, temperature, soil nutrient, and altitudinal regimes. By linking environmental descriptors to gene expression proxies, these networks accurately predicted metabolite yield across habitats and identified conditions that maximized hypericin production, illustrating how neural networks can connect external cues with biosynthetic gene cluster activity in medicinal plants. This approach enables researchers to optimize growth conditions for plants to maximize the yield of therapeutic compounds, establishing AI as a powerful tool in agricultural biotechnology and biopharmaceutical production. AI in synthetic biology and genomics The integration of AI with synthetic biology and genomics is another promising trend in bioprospecting. AI models can be used to design synthetic gene clusters that mimic natural biosynthetic pathways, allowing for the production of natural products in heterologous hosts (e.g., bacteria or yeast). By using AI to predict how genes interact within these synthetic pathways, researchers can optimize the production of bioactive compounds, even those that are difficult to produce in their native organisms [ 82 ]. A recent AI-driven study focused on using synthetic biology to reconstruct the biosynthetic pathway of dopamine, a plant-derived compound, in Escherichia coli . AI played a critical role in optimizing the expression of the enzymes involved in dopamine production [ 83 ]. By employing AI-guided pathway design and enzyme optimization, the researchers enhanced the production yield of dopamine in E. coli. This demonstrates how AI can improve the efficiency of heterologous synthesis, facilitating the scalable production of bioactive compounds in microbes. By optimizing the expression of the reconstructed pathway using AI models, the researchers increased the yield of the bioactive compound, showcasing the potential for AI to enhance natural product production through synthetic biology. AI for predicting gene expression and regulation in natural product biosynthesis Gene expression regulation plays a crucial role in determining the production of secondary metabolites in both plants and microorganisms. AI can be applied to predict the regulatory mechanisms that control the expression of biosynthetic genes, allowing for the rational manipulation of these pathways to enhance the production of bioactive compounds [ 54 ]. Machine learning algorithms, such as random forests (RF) and support vector machines (SVM), neural networks, and deep learning (NNs-DL), Weighted Gene Co-Expression Network Analysis (WGCNA), have been used to analyze transcriptomic data—data that reflect the expression levels of genes under different environmental or experimental conditions. By correlating gene expression patterns with the production of specific metabolites, these AI models can predict which genes are involved in the biosynthesis of bioactive compounds and how they are regulated. This enables targeted genetic engineering efforts to enhance metabolite production in both natural and synthetic systems. AI in high-throughput screening and compound optimization AI in High-Throughput Screening (HTS) High-throughput screening (HTS) is a cornerstone of modern drug discovery, including in the search for bioactive natural products. Traditional HTS involves testing thousands of compounds against specific biological targets to identify potential drug candidates. However, the sheer volume of natural compounds and the complexity of their interactions with biological systems present significant challenges for traditional HTS methods, which are time-consuming and resource-intensive. AI is now playing a critical role in enhancing HTS by improving the efficiency of compound selection and optimizing screening processes. AI-driven models can significantly reduce the time and cost associated with HTS by predicting which compounds are most likely to exhibit bioactivity, thus focusing experimental efforts on the most promising candidates. One notable application is virtual screening, where AI algorithms simulate the interaction of natural compounds with biological targets at the molecular level, enabling researchers to prioritize compounds for physical screening [ 84 ]. This approach has been applied in bioprospecting to identify potential inhibitors for enzymes involved in diseases such as cancer, infectious diseases, and neurodegenerative disorders [ 84 , 85 ]. Machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) algorithms are particularly effective in virtual screening because they can process large chemical datasets and learn patterns that indicate bioactivity. In terms of application, ML models have been utilized to predict the binding affinity of natural compounds to target proteins based on their chemical structure and known interactions [ 30 , 33 ]. These predictions enable researchers to focus on compounds with high predicted binding affinity, streamlining the drug discovery pipeline. In silico docking and virtual screening In silico docking is a computational method used to predict how small molecules, such as natural compounds, fit into the active sites of proteins. This process simulates the binding of compounds to biological targets, helping to identify potential drug candidates with high specificity and efficacy. AI enhances in silico docking by improving prediction accuracy and reducing the computational time needed to screen large natural product libraries. Deep learning models, like convolutional neural networks (CNNs), predict the three-dimensional structures of proteins and their interactions with natural compounds. These models learn from extensive datasets of protein-ligand interactions and can accurately estimate the binding affinity of novel compounds [ 86 ]. By leveraging these AI tools, researchers can quickly screen thousands of natural compounds and identify those with the highest potential for therapeutic use. For instance, AI-based in silico screening has identified natural product inhibitors of kinases, a class of enzymes involved in cancer progression. Through virtual screening, AI models predicted which natural compounds were most likely to inhibit kinase activity, discovering several promising anticancer agents [ 87 ]. This method accelerates the drug discovery process and reduces the need for costly and time-consuming laboratory experiments. AI for structure-based drug design and AlphaFold’ s growing impact on AI-driven drug discovery process Structure-based drug design (SBDD) is critical to drug discovery, particularly when targeting complex diseases such as cancer, neurodegenerative disorders, and infectious diseases. SBDD involves designing new compounds based on the three-dimensional structure of biological targets, typically proteins. AI is revolutionizing SBDD by predicting how natural compounds interact with these targets, facilitating the design of drugs that are more effective, selective, and stable. Recent advancements in AI-driven protein structure prediction, such as the development of AlphaFold [ 88 ], have had a significant impact on natural product drug discovery. AlphaFold, a deep learning-based model, can predict the 3D structure of proteins with remarkable accuracy, enabling researchers to identify how natural compounds can interact with specific protein targets. This has opened new avenues for structure-based design of natural product derivatives, allowing for the optimization of their binding affinity and pharmacokinetic properties. AlphaFold has transformed structural biology by predicting protein structures with near-experimental accuracy, thus filling critical knowledge gaps that previously hindered structure-based drug discovery [ 88 ]. By providing reliable three-dimensional models of proteins, even without crystallographic data, AlphaFold allows researchers to rapidly investigate ligand-binding sites, perform virtual screening, and refine hit compounds based on detailed structural insights. This synergy between accurate protein models and artificial intelligence greatly accelerates the early stages of drug discovery, where in vitro or in vivo assays are both costly and time-consuming. A recent illustration is Ren et al. [ 89 ], who integrated AlphaFold cyclin-dependent kinase 20 (CDK20) predictions into an AI-driven generative chemistry pipeline, ultimately discovering selective inhibitors for hepatocellular carcinoma after synthesizing only a handful of molecules. In parallel, Baselious et al. [ 90 ]. employed AlphaFold’s histone deacetylase 11 (HDAC11) structure, supplementing the predicted model with crucial cofactors and refining it via molecular dynamics to successfully dock known inhibitors. Together, these cases demonstrate how AlphaFold, when combined with machine learning–based design and optimization tools, can reduce development timelines and enhance the likelihood of identifying potent leads. AI in de Novo design of natural compound analogs De novo drug design refers to the process of creating new drug molecules from scratch, rather than modifying existing compounds. AI has emerged as a powerful tool for de novo design, especially in developing analogs of bioactive natural compounds. By using AI to explore chemical space and predict molecular properties, researchers can design novel analogs that retain the beneficial attributes of the original compound while enhancing its drug-like characteristics [ 54 ]. For instance, ML strategies are already guiding the design and prioritization of next-generation artemisinin derivatives. Kadioglu et al. [ 91 ]. assembled a library of 374 analogs and used a random forest platform trained on five cardiotoxicity endpoints to rank compounds before synthesis; the top seven scoring molecules demonstrated minimal hERG inhibition and no observable cardiotoxicity in zebrafish assays, illustrating how in-silico safety profiling can streamline hit selection. Complementing this safety-first approach, Araújo et al. [ 92 ] combined molecular electrostatic potential mapping, ligand–heme docking, and multivariate QSAR models (partial least squares and support vector regression) to propose N-11 azaartemisinin analogs with improved predicted potency; the best candidates inhibited Plasmodium falciparum growth at sub-micromolar concentrations. Together, these studies demonstrate that data-driven modeling can optimize both efficacy and safety profiles, accelerating the translation of artemisinin scaffolds into more potent and tolerable antimalarial agents. De novo design of bioactive molecules De novo drug design involves creating entirely new chemical entities, novel scaffolds or backbones that do not exist in current chemical libraries. Rather than modifying an existing molecule, AI-driven algorithms (e.g., generative adversarial networks [GANs], variational autoencoders [VAEs], or reinforcement learning models) are used to propose new molecular structures based on desired properties such as potency, selectivity, and favorable ADMET profiles [ 93 , 94 ]. In natural product research, de novo design can be compelling for exploring uncharted regions of chemical space, potentially yielding innovative scaffolds that offer unique mechanisms of action. Although Halicin is frequently cited as an AI-driven antibiotic discovery [ 16 ], it is important to note that it was identified by screening a pre-existing compound library using deep learning rather than being generated entirely de novo . Its case nonetheless exemplifies the power of AI to uncover potent, unexpected bioactivities. Small-Molecule TNIK Inhibitor: In contrast, Ren et a l [ 95 ] reported a de novo discovery process for a TNIK inhibitor that targets fibrosis in both preclinical and clinical models. By combining AI-driven virtual screening with iterative lead optimization, their pipeline generated novel scaffolds not previously found in chemical databases. Laboratory assays confirmed robust anti-fibrotic activity, positioning the compound for potential therapeutic development. The advantages of de n ovo design include expanding the search space beyond known chemical libraries and identifying completely novel scaffolds with unique biological activities. However, it also comes with limitations: These approaches are computationally demanding, and de novo hits are often synthetically challenging; therefore, extensive in vitro validation is essential to confirm feasibility, efficacy, and safety. Analog design of natural products Analog design—also known as lead optimization or scaffold hopping—involves modifying an existing natural product scaffold to enhance its pharmacological properties, such as potency, bioavailability, or safety. In this approach, AI tools (e.g., QSAR models and structure–activity relationship [SAR] analyses) help predict the effects of specific structural modifications. The analog design aims to retain the core bioactivity while incrementally improving the drug’s overall profile by building upon a proven molecular framework. Wong et al. [ 96 ]. Provide a recent example of how explainable deep learning can be used in a substructure-focused approach to identify new antibiotic leads. They tested over 39,000 compounds for antimicrobial activity and cytotoxicity, then trained ensembles of graph neural networks to predict which structures in a 12-million-compound library might display potent antibiotic properties with minimal human cell toxicity. Crucially, they employed an explainable AI method to isolate the chemical substructure associated with high predicted activity and low predicted cytotoxicity. By focusing on these substructures, Wong and colleagues discovered a structural class of antibiotics effective against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and vancomycin-resistant enterococci. This class also evaded substantial resistance development and showed efficacy in mouse models of MRSA infection. Although these scaffolds were previously underexplored, the team’s substructure-based screening and optimization process exemplifies an analog-like design philosophy in which AI identifies and refines key molecular motifs to yield improved antibiotic candidates. Analog design leverages known bioactive scaffolds, which reduces the risk of off-target effects and often allows for simpler synthetic routes compared to creating entirely new molecules. A major limitation, however, is that his approach depends on the availability of a tractable parent scaffold and may not achieve the same level of novelty as a de novo design. Case studies: AI in natural product drug discovery Case study 1: AI in anticancer compound discovery Natural products have long been a source of anticancer agents, with compounds such as paclitaxel, vinblastine, and camptothecin being widely used in cancer chemotherapy. However, the discovery of novel anticancer compounds from natural sources is becoming increasingly challenging due to the complexity of natural extracts and the limitations of traditional screening methods. AI offers a solution by enabling the efficient identification of natural compounds with anticancer properties through predictive modeling and virtual screening. In a notable case, AI models were used to identify novel anticancer compounds from marine sponges, a rich source of bioactive natural products. Using deep learning algorithms, researchers analyzed the chemical structures of marine sponge extracts and predicted which compounds were most likely to exhibit anticancer activity based on their structure-activity relationships (SARs). The AI models successfully identified several marine-derived compounds with potent anticancer activity, including inhibitors of cancer cell proliferation and inducers of apoptosis [ 97 ]. Moreover, AI-driven in silico docking was used to predict the binding interactions of these compounds with key cancer-related proteins, such as tyrosine kinases and DNA topoisomerases. By narrowing down the list of candidate compounds through virtual screening, researchers could focus on the most promising leads, significantly reducing the time and cost associated with traditional anticancer drug discovery [ 98 , 99 ]. Case study 2: AI and antimicrobial peptide discovery Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is a growing global health crisis, and there is an urgent need for new antimicrobial agents. Natural products, particularly antimicrobial peptides (AMPs), offer a promising solution. AMPs are small, naturally occurring peptides that exhibit broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity, making them potential candidates for developing new antibiotics. However, identifying and optimizing AMPs from natural sources is a challenging process, given the vast diversity of peptides and their complex mechanisms of action. AI has proven to be a powerful tool in accelerating AMP discovery. In a recent study, AI models were used to screen large datasets of natural peptides and predict their antimicrobial activity based on their physicochemical properties and sequence motifs [ 100 ]. The AI-driven screening process identified several novel AMPs from natural sources, including marine organisms and terrestrial fungi, which exhibited potent antimicrobial activity against drug-resistant bacterial strains. One of the most groundbreaking discoveries facilitated by AI was the identification of halicin , a synthetic product with broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity. Halicin was discovered using a deep learning model trained on a large dataset of chemical compounds. The AI model predicted that halicin, which was previously unstudied as an antibiotic, could inhibit bacterial growth by disrupting bacterial membrane function. Subsequent laboratory testing confirmed its efficacy against several drug-resistant bacteria, including Escherichia coli and Mycobacterium tuberculosis [ 16 ]. This discovery highlights the potential of AI to identify novel synthetic antimicrobial agents, particularly in the fight against antimicrobial resistance. Case study 3: AI in the discovery of anti-inflammatory compounds Chronic inflammation is implicated in numerous diseases, including autoimmune disorders, cardiovascular diseases, and cancer. Natural products, particularly those derived from plants, have been used for centuries in traditional medicine to treat inflammation. AI is now being applied to accelerate the discovery of new anti-inflammatory compounds from natural sources, especially by analyzing complex chemical profiles in plant extracts. Deep-learning and machine-learning pipelines have already uncovered potent anti-inflammatory leads from approved drug libraries and large virtual collections. Yasir and coworkers [ 101 ] used a convolutional neural network trained on known enzyme–ligand pairs to screen approximately 8,000 marketed or investigational compounds, and unexpectedly flagged vismodegib as a high-affinity cyclo-oxygenase-2 (COX-2) binder; docking and enzyme assays confirmed low-micromolar inhibition, revealing an unrecognized anti-inflammatory mechanism and illustrating the power of AI-driven drug repositioning. Complementing this target-directed strategy, Fry-Nartey et al. [ 102 ]. developed AICpred, an XGBoost-based web server trained on curated inhibitors of the Toll-like-receptor-4/MyD88 interface, a key trigger of cytokine-storm pathology. The model achieved an external accuracy of 0.99 and prioritized baricitinib, ibrutinib, and several natural-product scaffolds as probable TLR4 antagonists for experimental follow-up when applied to a diverse compound library. Together, these studies demonstrate how deep-learning classification, network integration, and structure-based validation can rapidly highlight new anti-inflammatory candidates by repurposing existing drugs or pinpointing understudied natural products, thereby reducing the time and cost of traditional bioassay-driven discovery. Case study 4: AI in bioprospecting for neuroprotective agents Neurodegenerative diseases, such as Alzheimer’s disease and Parkinson’s disease, are associated with the progressive loss of neuronal function and are major public health challenges. Natural products have been explored as potential neuroprotective agents due to their ability to modulate key biological pathways involved in neurodegeneration. AI tools are now routinely used to mine vast natural‑product libraries for compounds that can slow the molecular events driving Alzheimer’s, Parkinson’s, and other neurodegenerative disorders. A structure‑based, iterative‑learning platform recently combined docking data with a variational auto‑encoder to explore > 2 million small molecules, ultimately identifying a series of diaryl‑hydrazones that block the secondary nucleation of α‑synuclein two orders of magnitude more potently than previously known leads [ 103 ]. In another investigation, researchers combined a large protein‑interaction map of Parkinson’s disease with a graph neural network that merged chemical features, network context, and prediction uncertainty. The workflow isolated key functional clusters in the disease network, ranked the most influential proteins, and then nominated existing compounds, including among them dithiazanine, ceftolozane, α‑tocopherol, and medronic acid, that jointly act on multiple pathogenic pathways such as lysosomal failure, mitochondrial stress, synaptic loss, and neuroinflammation. The study shows that network‑aware deep learning can uncover repurposable, multi‑target drugs and may speed development of disease‑modifying treatments for Parkinson’s and other complex neurodegenerative disorders [ 104 ]. Together, these examples show that AI/ML techniques can rapidly triage chemical space, propose binding poses, and prioritize natural‑product scaffolds, turning what once required years of empirical screening into a matter of weeks while still delivering compounds that hold up under biological testing (Table 1 ) and Fig. 3 ). Full size image Chemical structures of representative drug candidates discovered or optimized with AI Challenges and limitations of AI, and the need for clinical validation Artificial intelligence tools can discover promising natural products in silico within days; however, each AI-generated lead must still navigate the conventional preclinical and clinical pipeline enforced by global regulators. The U.S. FDA and the EMA now provide specific guidance on how developers should document and validate AI models. Yet, they continue to require the same pharmacology, toxicology, and efficacy evidence mandated for any small-molecule drug. Data heterogeneity, limited high-quality reference sets, and the complexity of natural products further constrain algorithmic accuracy, emphasizing that rigorous bench work, cross-validation, and human trials remain indispensable. The FDA’s 2024 draft guidance frames AI outputs as supporting evidence and stresses the need for a “credibility assessment” before such models can influence regulatory decisions [ 106 ]. Similarly, the agency’s long-standing IND regulations still require full pharmacology and toxicology packages—including in vitro, rodent, and non-rodent studies—before first-in-human testing [ 106 ]. Europe applies comparable principles through its revised first-in-human guideline, highlighting additional risk-mitigation steps whenever novel computational methods drive candidate selection [ 107 ]. Recent policy analyses note that, although AI may accelerate dossier preparation, regulators will not waive any efficacy or safety endpoints for “digital” leads [ 108 , 109 ]. Despite this, there are several limitations, as machine-learning accuracy depends on data volume, balance, and chemical diversity; natural product libraries often fall short on all three counts [ 110 , 111 ]. Complex scaffolds introduce unusual ADME and polypharmacology profiles that algorithms trained largely on synthetic compounds may overlook [ 112 ]. Such gaps explain why in silico predictions of strong target binding can falter in vivo when off-target toxicity, metabolism, or immune responses intervene. From in silico hit to approved drug: After computational triage, candidates advance to the familiar sequence of in vitro target engagement, animal pharmacodynamics, dose-range finding, and formal GLP toxicity studies. Only then may sponsors file an IND and enter Phase I safety trials in healthy volunteers, followed by Phase II efficacy testing in 100–500 patients and large, multi-center Phase III confirmation [ 106 , 107 ]. Even post-approval, regulators expect ongoing Phase IV surveillance to capture rare adverse events or resistance mechanisms. Halicin is a good example, where a deep-learning screen of ~ 100 million compounds highlighted halicin as a broad-spectrum antibacterial. Laboratory assays confirmed its proton-motive-force disruption mechanism, and mouse models showed efficacy against drug-resistant A. baumannii and E. coli infections [ 16 ]. Despite this success, halicin must still complete formal toxicology and phased clinical trials before human use, underscoring that algorithms accelerate discovery but do not replace regulatory science. In summary, AI-driven pipelines can compress the hit-to-lead timeline but cannot waive the evidentiary thresholds that regulators impose. Accurate models rely on diverse, high-quality data; experimental pharmacology validates biological relevance; and phased clinical trials remain the ultimate arbiters of safety and efficacy. Bridging AI innovation with rigorous bench science will determine which computational discoveries become real-world medicines. A more in-depth look into the potential issues arising from the use of AI will be discussed below: Data availability and quality The effectiveness of AI models in bioprospecting depends heavily on the availability and quality of data. AI algorithms require large, high-quality datasets to learn and make accurate predictions. However, in the field of natural products, obtaining comprehensive and standardized data can be difficult for several reasons: Incomplete Datasets: Natural product libraries, particularly those derived from plants, microorganisms, and marine organisms, often lack complete information regarding chemical structures, biological activities, and mechanisms of action. Many natural products have not been fully characterized, resulting in gaps in the data that can negatively impact AI model performance [ 113 ]. Heterogeneous Data: Bioprospecting involves data from diverse fields, including genomics, metabolomics, cheminformatics, and traditional medicinal knowledge. These datasets are often heterogeneous, with format, quality, and completeness variations. Integrating these datasets into a cohesive platform for AI analysis is challenging and requires sophisticated data preprocessing and normalization techniques [ 114 ]. Biased data AI models can only make accurate predictions if trained on representative datasets. Datasets are often skewed toward positive examples (i.e., compounds that exhibit bioactivity), while negative data (i.e., compounds that do not show activity) are underrepresented, leading to potential overfitting and false-positive predictions. Efforts to improve data sharing, standardization, and the creation of comprehensive databases are crucial to overcoming these limitations. Publicly accessible natural product databases, such as the Natural Products Atlas and the Dictionary of Natural Products, provide valuable resources for training AI models. However, more collaborative initiatives are needed to ensure that datasets are comprehensive, reliable, and updated regularly [ 115 ]. FAIR-compliant natural product databases Open-access databases are key in improving the breadth and depth of data available to AI‐driven natural product discovery. Licensed collections are valuable yet restrict broad dissemination due to paywalls, potentially biasing research toward well‐studied compounds. In contrast, FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable)–compliant resources facilitate unencumbered access to structured molecular data, strengthening machine learning pipelines and promoting reproducible research. These open resources include COCONUT (Collection of Open Natural Products), LOTUS (Linked Open Data for Natural Products), and The Natural Products Atlas. COCONUT aggregates thousands of natural product structures with standardized identifiers to simplify cheminformatics workflows [ 115 ]. LOTUS links chemical entities to taxonomic and geographic data in a seamless open data format [ 116 ]. The Natural Products Atlas focuses on microbial natural products, offering curated data on structural diversity and metadata [ 110 ]. Collectively, these databases reduce duplication, enhance interoperability, and extend coverage to lesser-explored chemical scaffolds that may be overlooked in commercial libraries. The use of FAIR-compliant databases mitigates potential taxonomic or geographic bias, yielding more extensive and varied training sets for AI applications. This expanded diversity in chemical structures and associated metadata strengthens predictive models, increasing the likelihood of identifying novel or underexplored bioactive molecules. By consolidating extensive open-access resources under common data standards, these platforms not only expedite discovery but also foster transparent, global collaboration in the pursuit of new therapeutics from natural products. Model interpretability and complexity One of the major challenges in using AI, particularly deep learning models, in bioprospecting is the issue of interpretability. While deep learning models can achieve remarkable accuracy in predicting bioactivity and molecular interactions, they often operate as “black boxes,” meaning that the rationale behind their predictions is not easily interpretable by researchers [ 117 ]. This lack of interpretability is problematic for several reasons: Trust and Validation - In drug discovery, it is essential to understand the reasoning behind AI predictions to validate the findings. Researchers need to know which chemical features or structural motifs of a natural compound contribute to its predicted bioactivity. Without this knowledge, it is difficult to trust AI-driven results, particularly in biopharmaceutical applications where safety and efficacy are paramount. Regulatory Hurdles - For AI-predicted compounds to move forward in the drug development pipeline, regulatory agencies such as the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the European Medicines Agency (EMA) require transparent and interpretable data to assess the safety and effectiveness of new drug candidates. AI models that lack interpretability may face challenges in gaining regulatory approval [ 118 ]. Efforts to improve the interpretability of AI models are ongoing. For instance, explainable AI (XAI) techniques are being developed to provide insights into the decision-making process of AI models. These techniques aim to identify which features or patterns the model is using to make predictions, allowing researchers to better understand and validate the results [ 69 ]. Additionally, hybrid models that combine traditional statistical methods with AI algorithms can provide more interpretable results while maintaining high predictive accuracy [ 119 ]. Integration of multidisciplinary knowledge AI in bioprospecting requires integrating knowledge from multiple disciplines, including chemistry, biology, pharmacology, and data science. However, achieving effective collaboration between these fields poses a significant challenge. AI experts may not possess in-depth knowledge of natural product chemistry or biological pathways, while biologists and chemists may lack the technical expertise needed to develop and refine AI models. This lack of cross-disciplinary expertise can hinder the development and application of AI tools in bioprospecting. For instance, designing an AI model to predict bioactivity requires understanding both chemical structures and biological mechanisms, which may be outside the scope of expertise for a single researcher or team [ 120 ]. Additionally, integrating AI with traditional bioprospecting methods, such as extraction, isolation, and bioassays, requires collaboration between laboratory scientists and computational researchers. To address this issue, interdisciplinary training programs and collaborative research initiatives are needed to bridge the gap between AI and natural product research. Universities and research institutions are increasingly offering programs that combine AI with life sciences, allowing researchers to gain expertise in both fields. Furthermore, collaborative platforms and networks that bring together AI specialists and bioprospecting scientists can foster innovation and accelerate the development of AI-driven tools [ 121 ]. Ethical and environmental considerations The integration of AI in bioprospecting, while transformative, also raises critical ethical and environmental concerns. Chief among these is the potential for overexploitation of natural resources, particularly in ecologically sensitive and biodiversity-rich regions such as tropical rainforests and coral reefs. As AI accelerates the identification of valuable bioactive compounds, it may inadvertently increase demand for rare or endangered species, thereby placing additional pressure on fragile ecosystems and contributing to biodiversity loss [ 122 ]. Intellectual Property and Access to Genetic Resources: The use of AI in bioprospecting raises questions about intellectual property (IP) rights and access to genetic resources. Many of the natural compounds identified through AI-driven bioprospecting come from indigenous knowledge or biodiversity-rich regions in developing countries. Ensuring that these regions benefit from the commercialization of natural products is essential for ethical bioprospecting. International agreements, such as the Nagoya Protocol on Access and Benefit-sharing, provide a framework for ensuring that benefits are shared equitably, but enforcement and compliance remain challenging [ 123 ]. Sustainability in Drug Discovery: The overreliance on AI-driven bioprospecting must be balanced with sustainable practices that ensure the conservation of ecosystems. AI can play a role in promoting sustainability [ 124 ] by predicting the environmental impact of natural product harvesting and guiding researchers toward more sustainable sources of bioactive compounds. For example, AI can be used to identify microorganisms or plants that can be cultivated sustainably, reducing the need for wild harvesting [ 125 , 126 ]. To address these ethical and environmental concerns, bioprospecting initiatives must adhere to principles of sustainable use and fair access, ensuring that biodiversity is conserved and that the local community benefits from the commercialization of natural resources. Future trends and opportunities in AI-driven bioprospecting As AI continues to evolve, several emerging trends and opportunities are shaping the future of AI-driven bioprospecting for biopharmaceutical purposes. These trends focus on improving the efficiency of natural product discovery, integrating AI with new technologies, and promoting sustainability in drug development. AI for personalized medicine and precision bioprospecting AI platforms are beginning to link natural products’ enormous chemical diversity with individual patients’ molecular heterogeneity. By integrating genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, and metabolomics, machinelearning models can uncover patterns that predict how a particular compound will be absorbed, transformed, and tolerated in a defined subgroup [ 127 , 128 ]. Random forests, support vector machines, and deep neural networks have already been used to stratify patients based on mutation status, pathway activity, or metabolic fingerprints, thereby matching naturalproduct candidates to those most likely to benefit [ 129 , 130 ]. At the same time, virtual screening engines powered by graph neural networks or transformer architectures can interrogate large naturalproduct biobanks and highlight molecules whose predicted binding profiles align with patient-specific targets [ 131 ]. Generative models—including variational autoencoders and generative adversarial networks—extend this capability by proposing novel derivatives optimized for potency, safety, or pharmacokinetics in precisely defined populations [ 20 ]. Proof-of-concept studies demonstrate the clinical promise of this approach. Yang et al. [ 132 ] used an ensemble learning pipeline to examine colorectal cancer genomes. They showed that tumors carrying wild-type KRAS responded far better to curcumin than those with oncogenic mutations, paving the way for the genotype-directed use of this turmeric-derived polyphenol. In breast cancer plasma, Yagin et al. [ 133 ] coupled explainable AI with targeted metabolomics to nominate a short panel of lipids that discriminates disease subtypes and may guide dietarybased interventions. A separate structure-based workflow focused on Saussurea lappa identified dehydrocostus lactone and mokkolactone as potent binders of the EGFRL858R mutant that drives non-smallcell lung cancer; subsequent cell assays confirmed anti-proliferative activity, illustrating how naturalproduct libraries can be mined for mutation-specific inhibitors [ 134 ]. Multi-omics integration remains central to these advances. Genomic features such as BRCA1/2 status can flag patients who will metabolize or respond to certain polyphenols [ 135 ]. Predictive models trained on curated drug–target datasets now forecast binding to mutant proteins or alternative conformations with everhigher accuracy [ 136 ]. Together, these AI-driven strategies are converting naturalproduct discovery from a trial-and-error enterprise into a data-guided process that tailors therapies to the molecular signatures of each patient cohort, marking an important step toward precision bioprospecting. Conclusion Artificial Intelligence (AI) is transforming bioprospecting by providing unprecedented opportunities to discover bioactive natural compounds for biopharmaceutical purposes. By integrating AI with traditional methods, researchers can explore natural ecosystems more efficiently, analyze complex chemical structures, and predict the therapeutic potential of natural products. AI-driven tools—such as machine learning, deep learning, and cheminformatics—have already proven valuable in identifying novel anticancer, antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective agents from natural sources. However, to fully realize AI’s potential in bioprospecting, challenges related to data quality, model interpretability, and interdisciplinary integration must be addressed. Ethical and environmental considerations are also crucial to ensure that AI-driven bioprospecting is conducted sustainably and equitably. Looking ahead, emerging trends such as personalized medicine, the exploration of underexplored ecosystems, and the integration of AI with synthetic biology will continue to drive innovation in natural product drug discovery. With its ability to accelerate therapeutic discovery, AI is poised to play a pivotal role in the next generation of biopharmaceutical research. Continued collaboration among scientists, technologists, and ethicists is essential to capitalize on these opportunities. By fostering interdisciplinary partnerships and advancing AI technologies, the biopharmaceutical industry can harness the full potential of AI-driven bioprospecting to benefit global health.

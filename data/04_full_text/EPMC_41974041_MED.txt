FAQ INTRODUCTION hepatitis E virus (HEV) is an important cause of chronic hepatitis in immunocompromised individuals such as transplant recipients, persons living with human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infection, and patients with hematological malignancy. 1–3 These patients may fail to clear HEV infection despite reduction of immunosuppression. 4 Such chronic infections can progress to liver fibrosis and cirrhosis if left untreated. 2,5 Swine-related zoonotic Paslahepevirus balayani genotypes 3 and 4 cause most chronic hepatitis E cases, while Rocahepevirus ratti genotype 1 (rat hepatitis E; rHEV) is an emerging cause of chronic hepatitis E in immunocompromised hosts. 6–11 The main treatment option for chronic hepatitis E is oral ribavirin, a guanosine analogue. 12,13 Sustained virological response (SVR) is achievable in nearly 90% of treated individuals, although some patients require multiple courses. 14 Two limitations of ribavirin therapy are hemolytic anemia and treatment failure. Anemia can be severe, leading to transfusion-dependence, dose reduction, or treatment discontinuation. 12,14 Mutations in the RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) gene may emerge during ribavirin treatment and are correlated with non-response. 15 There are few alternatives for patients who are intolerant or unresponsive to ribavirin. Pegylated interferon α has been used but carries the risk of organ rejection and myelosuppression. 16 Sofosbuvir was promising in preclinical studies but has proved disappointing in actual clinical practice. 17–19 There is an unmet need for an alternative agent for chronic hepatitis E, and this has been identified as a major translational challenge in the field. 20 Repositioning licensed antivirals is a promising strategy for identifying therapeutic candidates for this condition. The coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic offers a unique opportunity due to the approval of new antivirals active against severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2). One of these is molnupiravir (EIDD-2801), which is a prodrug of β-D-N 4 -hydroxycytidine (NHC; EIDD-1931), a cytidine analogue. 21 NHC in its triphosphate form is incorporated into RNA by viral RdRp. This directs incorporation of either G or A into the nascent RNA strand, resulting in lethal mutagenesis. 22 Viral RdRp is relatively conserved, making NHC active against many RNA viruses. 23–25 Furthermore, other cytidine analogues have already been shown to be effective against HEV in vitro. 26–28 As molnupiravir is commercially available in an oral form with proven safety in humans, it has a shorter pathway to off-label clinical use for hepatitis E. For these reasons, we investigated molnupiravir in HEV infection using in vitro and immunocompromised animal HEV models. METHODS HEV isolates and infectious clones Fecal filtrates containing viral loads of 10 8 copies/mL were derived from a solid organ transplant (SOT) recipient with chronic HEV genotype 4 infection and rats infected with the SRN rHEV strain (methodology in Supplemental Methods, https://links.lww.com/HC9/C322 ). Sequences of both strains are available on GenBank (accession nos: MK016529 and MG813928, respectively). We have validated the presence of viable virus in these filtrates in cell culture and animal models previously. 29,30 SRN is a street rat-derived subtype I.a rHEV strain that is phylogenetically related to rHEV isolates from humans in Spain. 10,31 The full-length HEV genotype 3 infectious cDNA clone Kernow-C1 p6; GenBank accession no. JQ679013 was used in this study. 32 Cells, culture media, and antivirals Human hepatoma cell line PLC/PRF/5 (ATCC CRL-8024, LGC Standards, Wesel, Germany) and primary rat hepatocytes (PRH) were used in this study. PRHs were isolated by a 2-step perfusion method. Briefly, a catheter was placed in the portal vein, and the inferior vena cava was cut. Livers were perfused with Hanks' Balanced Salt Solution (HBSS, Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc, USA) supplemented with collagenase IV (Sigma-Aldrich, St Louis, MO, USA). After dissociation, cells were filtered through 70 μm filters. PRHs were further separated by low-speed centrifugation and seeded to a density of 10 6 cells/mL. PLC/PRF/5 cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM, GIBCO, New York, USA) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), 1% dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO), 1% penicillin–streptomycin solution, 2.5 g/mL amphotericin B and 30 mM MgCl 2 (Sigma-Aldrich, St Louis, MO, USA) as described previously. 29,33 For in vitro experiments, ribavirin (99.8% purity), NHC (the active form of molnupiravir, at 99.5% purity), and sofosbuvir (99.97% purity) were obtained commercially (MedChemExpress, Shanghai, China) and diluted in DMSO according to manufacturer instructions. For in vivo experiments, pharmaceutical-grade ribavirin capsules (TSH Biopharm, Taipei, Taiwan) and molnupiravir capsules (Merck, Rahway, USA) were procured from the Department of Pharmacy, Queen Mary Hospital, Hong Kong. Cytotoxicity and antiviral assays A cell counting kit-8 (CCK8) assay was performed to evaluate the cytotoxicity of molnupiravir, ribavirin, and sofosbuvir (see Supplemental Methods, https://links.lww.com/HC9/C322 ). For antiviral assays, naive PLC/PRF/5 cells were seeded into 12 or 24-well plates and incubated in the previously described media until hyper-confluent. PRHs were pre-seeded for 24 hours at 10 6 cells/mL. On the day of infection, cells were washed with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and incubated for 4 hours with media supplemented with molnupiravir (1, 10, 50, and 100 μM), ribavirin (10, 50, 100, and 200 μM), sofosbuvir (0.1, 1, 5, and 10 μM), molnupiravir and sofosbuvir in combination (50/10, 10/10, and 1/10 μM), ribavirin and sofosbuvir in combination (100/10, 50/10, and 10/10 μM), or no-drug control (PBS). Media was removed and replaced with 500 μL of fecal filtrate (MOI: 1 genome equivalent per cell) from HEV genotype 4 (for PLC/PRF/5 cell lines) or rHEV (for PRHs) and incubated for 2 hours at room temperature. The inoculum was then removed, the cells washed with PBS, and replaced with antiviral-containing or no-drug control media and incubated at 37 °C and 5% CO 2 . Half of the supernatant was harvested and replaced with fresh media every 2–4 days. Plasmid Kernow-C1 p6 was linearized at the MluI site for RNA transcription. Capped transcripts were synthesized by the MEGAscript T7 kit (Invitrogen, Massachusetts, USA). In vitro-transcribed RNA was transfected into PLC/PRF/5 cells in 12-well plates using Lipofectamine 3000 Reagent (Invitrogen). The transfection solution contained 120 μL Opti-MEM (Invitrogen) and 10 μL transcription mixture containing 1200 ng of in vitro-transcribed RNA (0.3 μL), 3 μL Lipofectamine 3000 reagents, and 4 μL P3000 reagents. Then, media containing varying concentrations of molnupiravir, ribavirin, or sofosbuvir as described above were added. Wells without antiviral drugs served as experimental controls. Animal model of chronic hepatitis E In vivo experiments were approved by the Committee on the Use of Live Animals in Teaching and Research of The University of Hong Kong (protocol: 6052-22). Specific-pathogen-free female 6–8-week-old Sprague–Dawley rats (Rattus norvegicus ) were obtained from the Center for Comparative Medical Research of The University of Hong Kong. Rats had access to pellet feed and water ad libitum. Rats were fed a cocktail of immunosuppressants by oral gavage: prednisolone 10 mg/kg/d, tacrolimus 7.5 mg/kg/d, and mycophenolate mofetil 30 mg/kg/d. After 10 days of immunosuppression, rats were infected intravenously with fecal filtrate containing 2.5×10 5 copies of the SRN rHEV strain. Immunosuppression was continued after infection. At 7 days post-infection (dpi), rats were randomly divided into 4 groups (n=14 per group): ribavirin-treated (50 mg/kg/d), molnupiravir 250 mg/kg/d-treated, molnupiravir 400 mg/kg/d-treated, and untreated controls. Antivirals were administered by oral gavage together with immunosuppressants. The molnupiravir doses (400 and 250 mg/kg/d) were based on pharmacokinetic data in rats provided by drug developers. 34 Molnupiravir doses &gt;500 mg/kg/d corresponding to margins of exposure of 2–4× NHC exposure at recommended human dose (RHD), are associated with toxicity in female rats; therefore, 2 dose levels below this were chosen for this study. The 250 mg/kg/d dose corresponds to NHC exposure below that at RHD, and the 400 mg/kg/d dose was chosen to approximately correspond to the RHD. 34,35 The ribavirin dose was higher than those used in previously published rat models of hepatitis E because of our experience of relatively poor efficacy at 30 mg/kg/d. 30,36 For sample size calculation, we assumed that molnupiravir reduces mean stool rHEV virus shedding by 1 log 10 copies/mL compared with untreated animals. Based on this, the optimal sample size was 14 animals per group (α=0.05, power=90%). The experiment was split into mini-experiments separated by several weeks to ensure reproducibility. 37 Fecal samples were collected from each animal twice a week, and serum samples were collected once a week for measurement of rHEV RNA. Liver function tests were not monitored because we have previously shown that they are typically normal in this hepatitis E model. 30 Rats were monitored up to 35 dpi for humane endpoints (Supplemental Table S1, https://links.lww.com/HC9/C322 ). HEV viral load measurement Total nucleic acid was extracted from 200 μL of fecal filtrate or serum using the EZ1 Virus Mini Kit v2.0 (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). RNA was extracted from cell culture supernatant and homogenized rat liver tissues using the RNA extraction and purification kit (Guangzhou Dongsheng Biotech, Guangzhou, China) and RNeasy Mini kit (Qiagen), respectively. Viral loads were determined by validated quantitative real-time reverse transcription–polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) assays for HEV and rHEV using published protocols. 8,38 Next-generation sequencing Single-nucleotide variants (SNVs) arising in viral populations in feces or liver tissue during antiviral therapy were assessed as described previously with modifications. 39 RNA was extracted from rat stool or liver samples using the EZ1 Virus Mini Kit v2.0 (Qiagen) and underwent tiled primer PCR using primer pairs spanning the rHEV genome (Supplemental Table S2, https://links.lww.com/HC9/C322 ). Deep sequencing was performed using the Oxford Nanopore MinION platform. Bioinformatic analysis was performed as described previously with modifications. 39 Detailed protocols for library preparation, sequencing, and bioinformatic analysis are included in the Supplemental Methods, https://links.lww.com/HC9/C322 . Histology, immunohistochemistry, and immunofluorescence staining At 14 dpi, infected cell culture monolayers were detached using EDTA trypsin and fixed onto cover slips for immunofluorescence staining using diluted murine antisera containing polyclonal antibodies against HEV capsid protein, followed by Alexa Fluor 488-conjugated anti-mouse IgG secondary antibody (A-11008, Thermo Fisher Scientific). Rat liver tissues were fixed in 10% neutral buffered formalin, embedded in paraffin, and sectioned. The 4 μm-thick sections were de-paraffinized and then underwent hematoxylin and eosin (H&amp;E) staining and immunohistochemical examination as described previously. 30 Detailed protocols are included in the Supplemental Material, https://links.lww.com/HC9/C322 . Slides were examined by 2 veterinary pathologists blinded to treatment groups. Statistical analysis Group means were compared using an independent Student t test and the Kruskal–Wallis test with the Dunn multiple comparisons test. p &lt;0.05 was considered statistically significant. Charts were plotted using Prism (GraphPad, Boston, USA). RESULTS Effects of antiviral drugs on HEV infection in vitro Cytotoxicity of molnupiravir, ribavirin, and sofosbuvir was assessed in PLC/PRF/5 cells and PRHs. Neither molnupiravir nor sofosbuvir had discernible negative effects on cell viability in either cell line ( Figure 1A ). PRHs infected with the SRN rHEV strain showed significant antiviral responses in molnupiravir-treated cells at all concentrations, but this was not observed in ribavirin or sofosbuvir-treated cells ( Figure 1B ). We then evaluated the antiviral effect of combinations of submaximal concentrations of molnupiravir and sofosbuvir. The combination of molnupiravir (50 μM) and sofosbuvir (10 μM) was equivalent to single-use molnupiravir (50 μM). In contrast, the ribavirin–sofosbuvir combination was significantly superior to single-use ribavirin ( Figure 1B ). Immunofluorescence staining of HEV ORF2 protein expression in PRHs infected with rHEV showed a reduction in fluorescent signals in cells treated with ribavirin and molnupiravir, but not sofosbuvir ( Figure 1C ). We further validated the effect of molnupiravir in human hepatoma cells infected with HEV genotypes known to cause chronic hepatitis E. In PLC/PRF/5 cells transfected with the infectious clone Kernow-p6 or infected with patient-derived HEV genotype 4, both molnupiravir and ribavirin demonstrated consistent virus load reduction, while the impact of sofosbuvir was more variable ( Figures 1D, E ). DISCUSSION There is a long-standing need for an effective alternative to ribavirin for chronic hepatitis E. Cytidine analogues have been shown to be active in vitro against HEV. Debing et al first demonstrated that 2′-C-methylcytidine (2CMC) had modest antiviral activity against the distantly related cutthroat trout virus. 26 This was subsequently confirmed in HEV cDNA infectious clone models with a high barrier for resistance. 27 Here, we show that this effectiveness extends to NHC and its prodrug molnupiravir. Antiviral efficacy in cell culture does not consistently extend to clinical efficacy. For example, sofosbuvir exerts a robust antiviral effect against Kernow-C1 p6 but has generally proven disappointing in clinical practice. 17,19 Animal models are more clinically relevant, but convenient small animal models of hepatitis E are not universally available. We were able to confirm the activity of molnupiravir in both in vitro and in vivo models. In rats, we found that molnupiravir dosed at a higher dose point of 400 mg/kg/d was more effective than the lower dose in rats. This indicates that doses lower than the RHD (800 mg twice daily) are unlikely to be effective when used alone in HEV infection. Exposure-response relationships in pilot studies will be required to determine the optimal dose for HEV infection, but the RHD is a good starting point as safety has been established, at least for short-term administration. 40 Safety of longer molnupiravir courses in humans is unknown. Repeat dose toxicity studies, genotoxicity studies, and our preclinical evaluation in rats are reassuring in this respect. 34 The drug developer has also announced that molnupiravir is not carcinogenic in Tg-rasH2 mice after 6 months of administration. 41 Dogs develop reversible myelosuppression when given prolonged courses of high-dose molnupiravir, and this is a theoretical concern in humans. 35 However, anemia and myelosuppression are also encountered frequently with ribavirin and pegylated interferon. Given these uncertainties, a lower dose of molnupiravir in combination with an alternate agent may be a valid strategy. We did find partial efficacy and enhanced mutagenesis even at the lower dose point to support such an approach. As there is data indicating antagonism between 2CMC and ribavirin, sofosbuvir–molnupiravir combinations were evaluated in this study, but we were not able to demonstrate in vitro evidence to support an evaluation in rats. 27 Although we were able to demonstrate enhanced mutagenesis with ribavirin and molnupiravir, our sequencing strategy led to a significant loss of coverage for animals receiving antivirals. We were also unable to observe mutations when viral loads were falling due to sensitivity issues. We also cannot exclude mutations leading to molnupiravir non-responsiveness in the 250 mg/kg/d group. Methods that are more sensitive to minor genomic variants are required to elucidate molnupiravir-induced mutagenesis when viral loads are declining. We acknowledge that our animal model is more representative of the acute phase of HEV infection in transplant recipients rather than chronic hepatitis E. We cannot exclude differences in effectiveness in chronic hepatitis E (defined as viremia &gt;3 mo), which is an immunologically distinct entity with CD8 T-cell exhaustion. 42 The duration of treatment in a true chronic infection scenario would require further evaluation. However, there is accumulating rationale for antiviral initiation in acutely infected transplant recipients because of worsening functional T-cell exhaustion with prolonged infection. 3,42 In conclusion, we demonstrate the possible clinical utility of molnupiravir for chronic hepatitis E. Although this will require further clinical evaluation from a safety and effectiveness perspective, the commercial availability of oral molnupiravir in a readily absorbed form is its major advantage over experimental alternatives.
